# Narrono Core Rules Reference
## Resolution Methods
MethodDie UsedWhen to UseEffect
DirectHighestWhen acting in alignment with a traitHigher chance of success but greater risk
BalancedMiddleWhen no traits strongly applyMost consistent results
CautiousLowestWhen acting against trait tendenciesLower chance of success but reduced risk

Method Selection Guidelines

  • Direct: Use when the action clearly aligns with character traits
  • Balanced: Use when multiple traits have mixed relevance
  • Cautious: Use when going against character tendencies or in unfamiliar territory
## Understanding Results
ResultValueOutcome
Success5-6Achieve intention, strengthen position, add beneficial story element
Partial Success3-4Achieve intention with cost, face minor complication, create mixed opportunity
Setback1-2Miss intention, face major complication, create significant story impact

Dynamic Outcome Interpretation

After Success (5-6):

  • Grant the intention
  • Create future complications
  • Plant seeds for future challenges
  • Show how success changes the setting

After Partial Success (3-4):

  • Grant intention with meaningful cost
  • Shape costs to drive character development
  • Use costs to introduce new story elements
  • Connect costs to existing complications

After Setback (1-2):

  • Acknowledge the mechanical failure
  • Transform failure into opportunity
  • Reveal hidden information through failure
  • Create alternative paths forward
## The Compensation Principle

When dice create extreme results (multiple successes or failures in a row):

  1. Acknowledge the mechanical outcome
  2. Consider the narrative momentum
  3. Introduce balancing elements
  4. Create new opportunities

After Multiple Successes:

  • Introduce longer-term complications
  • Create tension between different successes
  • Show unintended consequences of victory
  • Raise stakes for future challenges

After Multiple Setbacks:

  • Reveal new information through failure
  • Create unexpected advantages
  • Introduce helpful NPCs or resources
  • Show character growth through adversity
## Asset States
StateEffectRecovery
FreshFull benefits-
DegradedLimited use with complicationsMinor narrative action
DepletedTemporarily unusableSignificant narrative action

Common Degradation Triggers

  • Multiple uses in same scene
  • Using against asset nature
  • Failed high-stakes action
  • Environmental damage

Recovery Requirements

  • Fresh → Degraded: Basic maintenance
  • Degraded → Depleted: Major repair
  • Depleted → Fresh: Complete overhaul
## Relationship States
StateEffectRecovery
StrongFull benefits and support-
StrainedLimited benefits with complicationsMinor positive interaction
BrokenTemporarily unusableMajor story event
TransformedChanged into new typeSignificant narrative development

Strain Points (0-5)

  • 0-2 points: Strained but Functional
  • 3-4 points: Severely Strained
  • 5+ points: Breaking Point

Strain Triggers

  • Direct betrayal: +2 points
  • Opposing actions: +1 point
  • Failed support: +1 point
  • Secret revealed: +1-2 points (based on severity)
## Shared Story Pool

Setup

  • Each player adds one d6
  • GF adds two d6s
  • Place dice where all can reach

Usage Options

Players can use dice from pool to:

  • Add Complexity: Introduce complication
  • Establish Relationships: Create connection
  • Create Opportunities: Add story element
  • Enhance Rolls: Add die to personal pool

GF Usage

Use pool dice to:

  • Add complexity when players are succeeding too easily
  • Create opportunities when players are struggling
  • Adjust scene difficulty dynamically
  • Balance individual and group challenges

# Setting Management Panel
# Narrono Setting Management Reference
## Five Axes Quick Reference
AxisOptionsInfluences
Power ScalePersonal, Local, Regional, GlobalScope of action, relationship reach, asset scale
SupernaturalNone, Hidden, Open, DominantKnowledge sources, conflict types, asset nature
TechnologyLimited, Moderate, Advanced, FantasticInformation flow, asset capabilities, conflict resolution
Social StructureTribal, Hierarchical, Merit, FluidRelationship types, status dynamics, asset access
Conflict TypeDirect, Social, Economic, IdeologicalResolution methods, relationship tensions, asset usage

Axis Interactions

  • Power + Social: Determines authority structures
  • Supernatural + Technology: Shapes knowledge systems
  • Conflict + Power: Defines resolution scope
  • Social + Conflict: Creates relationship dynamics
  • Technology + Power: Establishes communication patterns
## Setting Evolution Tracking

Element Changes

Track these ongoing developments:

  • New groups emerging within existing structures
  • Relationships between factions shifting
  • Resources becoming available or scarce
  • Knowledge spreading or becoming restricted

Internal Axis Shifts

Changes within an axis choice:

  • Power structures reorganizing within their scale
  • Supernatural forces finding new expressions
  • Technology developing within its level
  • Social structures adapting while maintaining their nature
  • Conflict patterns evolving within their type

Axis Transitions

Major changes moving from one axis choice to another:

  • Require significant story events
  • Should feel earned through play
  • Create lasting consequences
  • Usually happen gradually
## Setting Evolution Triggers

Minor Triggers (Element Changes)

  • Character actions affecting local power
  • Discovery of limited resources
  • Formation of new relationships
  • Development of specialized knowledge
  • Creation of new assets

Major Triggers (Axis Shifts)

  • Multiple minor changes accumulating
  • Critical story events
  • Group decisions with far-reaching impact
  • Significant resource discoveries/depletions
  • Technological breakthroughs
  • Social upheavals
  • Supernatural manifestations
## Faction Management

Faction Tracking

For each significant faction, track:

  • Power Base: Resources, territory, influence
  • Key Relationships: Allies, rivals, dependencies
  • Goals: Short-term and long-term objectives
  • Methods: How they pursue their goals
  • Assets: What they control or can access

Faction Evolution

  • Growth: Expanding influence or resources
  • Decline: Losing power or territory
  • Transformation: Changing methods or goals
  • Alliance: Forming new relationships
  • Conflict: Developing new rivalries
## Location Management

Location Elements

For each significant location, track:

  • Axis Expression: How each axis manifests
  • Power Centers: Who controls what
  • Resources: What's available or scarce
  • Tensions: Ongoing conflicts or issues
  • Opportunities: Potential developments

Location Evolution

  • Physical Changes: Environmental developments
  • Social Changes: Population or cultural shifts
  • Power Changes: Authority transitions
  • Resource Changes: Availability fluctuations
  • Connection Changes: Access or isolation
## Axis Shift Implementation

Gradual Change Process

  1. Establish current state clearly
  2. Show signs of potential change
  3. Create opportunities for character influence
  4. Let consequences ripple outward

Sudden Shift Process

  1. Identify the catalyzing event
  2. Determine immediate consequences
  3. Create adaptation challenges
  4. Establish new status quo
  5. Track ripple effects

Shift Documentation

For each axis shift, record:

  • Previous State: The starting position
  • Catalyzing Events: What triggered the change
  • New State: The resulting position
  • Immediate Effects: Direct consequences
  • Long-term Implications: Future developments

# Character Management Panel
# Narrono Character Management Reference
## Trait Development Guidelines

Origin (Social Structure)

Development paths:

  • Develop new connections within social framework
  • Navigate changing status and position
  • Face challenges to traditional roles
  • Build or strain relationships

Drive (Conflict Type)

Development paths:

  • Adapt methods to new challenges
  • Discover alternative approaches
  • Face conflicting motivations
  • Evolve goals and methods

Goal (Power Scale)

Development paths:

  • Expand or focus ambitions
  • Deal with changing scope
  • Navigate new power dynamics
  • Adjust to achieved objectives
## Trait Expansion Opportunities

Origin Enhancement

  • Add new relationships within original context
  • Develop specialized knowledge or authority
  • Create sub-groups or factions

Example: A "Mountain Clan Warrior" might develop "Voice of the Eastern Peaks"

Drive Refinement

  • Specify particular methods or approaches
  • Add secondary objectives
  • Connect to new conflict types

Example: "Control Trade Routes" might refine to "Master of the Silk Road"

Goal Development

  • Shift between power scales
  • Add intermediate objectives
  • Create parallel pursuits

Example: "Unite the Clans" might develop "Through Ancient Traditions"

## Asset Recovery Methods

Physical Assets

  • Fresh → Degraded: Basic maintenance, cleaning, minor repairs
  • Degraded → Depleted: Major repair, significant resources, specialist help
  • Depleted → Fresh: Complete overhaul, replacement, rare materials

Social Assets

  • Fresh → Degraded: Minor reputation management, small favors, token gestures
  • Degraded → Depleted: Social damage control, public amends, significant favors
  • Depleted → Fresh: Complete social reset, major status change, transformative action

Knowledge Assets

  • Fresh → Degraded: Update needed, minor research, consultation
  • Degraded → Depleted: Major revision required, significant study, expert guidance
  • Depleted → Fresh: Complete relearning, fundamental discovery, paradigm shift

Supernatural Assets

  • Fresh → Degraded: Power recharge, minor ritual, energy source
  • Degraded → Depleted: Ritual restoration, significant sacrifice, power source
  • Depleted → Fresh: Major supernatural event, fundamental transformation, cosmic alignment
## Relationship Evolution

State Changes

  • Strong → Strengthened: Through shared successes, mutual support, aligned goals
  • Strained → Tested: By conflicts, competing interests, external pressures
  • Broken → Damaged: By major betrayals, fundamental disagreements, opposing actions
  • Transformed → Changed: Into new relationship types through significant events

Recovery Methods

  • Minor recovery (-1 strain): Successful joint action, small personal sacrifice, public support
  • Major recovery (-2 strain): Significant personal risk, major resource commitment, critical story support
  • Complete reset (clear all strain): Relationship transformation, life-changing event, setting-scale impact

Development Paths

  • Deepening existing bonds through shared experiences
  • Forming new connections through story opportunities
  • Resolving old conflicts through narrative resolution
  • Transforming relationships through character growth
## Character Goal Tracking

Short-term Goals

  • Tied to immediate story arcs
  • Achievable within 1-3 sessions
  • Connected to character traits
  • Create immediate story opportunities

Medium-term Goals

  • Span multiple story arcs
  • Achievable within a story phase
  • Develop character traits
  • Create ongoing narrative tension

Long-term Goals

  • Span entire campaigns
  • Represent character's ultimate aims
  • May evolve through play
  • Create major story developments

Goal Integration

  • Connect to setting elements
  • Involve other characters
  • Utilize assets and relationships
  • Create meaningful choices
## Group Dynamics Management

Balance Spotlight Time

  • Track active participation
  • Create opportunities for quiet players
  • Connect character moments to group goals
  • Use relationship dynamics to involve multiple characters

Create Trait Synergies

  • Identify complementary traits
  • Create situations requiring multiple approaches
  • Highlight how different traits address the same problem
  • Show how traits can support each other

Encourage Asset Sharing

  • Create situations where assets complement each other
  • Show benefits of pooling resources
  • Create recovery opportunities through cooperation
  • Develop shared assets through joint actions

Build Group Narrative

  • Create shared goals that require all characters
  • Develop interconnected relationship webs
  • Show how individual actions affect the group
  • Create situations that highlight group identity

# Session Management Panel
# Narrono Session Management Reference
## Scene Creation Framework

The Three-Layer Approach

1. Setting Layer

Establish the context:

  • Reference relevant axis elements
  • Show setting constraints
  • Establish scene context
  • Present setting-specific details

Example: "In this Merit-based, Advanced Tech setting, the corporate lobby's security scanners hum quietly. Your degraded Access Card flickers, struggling with the latest encryption update."

2. Character Integration

Connect to characters:

  • Link to relevant traits
  • Show how complications manifest
  • Present growth opportunities
  • Connect to character goals

Example: "As a former Security Specialist, you recognize the system's weaknesses, but your Corporate Outcast status makes official access tricky."

3. Options Layer

Present meaningful choices:

  • Multiple viable approaches
  • Connect choices to traits and assets
  • Show immediate and long-term implications
  • Make each choice meaningful

Example: "You could use your security expertise directly (Direct roll, but risks recognition), try standard corporate protocols (Balanced roll), or rely on social engineering (Cautious roll)."

## Session Pacing Guidelines

Session Structure

A well-paced session typically includes:

  • Opening Scene: Establish situation and immediate goals
  • Rising Action: 2-3 scenes of increasing complexity
  • Turning Point: A major decision or revelation
  • Resolution: Consequences and outcomes
  • Denouement: Character moments and future hooks

Scene Transitions

Effective transitions between scenes:

  • Connect to character decisions
  • Show passage of time
  • Change location or perspective
  • Introduce new elements
  • Escalate tension or provide contrast

Pacing Adjustments

When to adjust pacing:

  • Speed up: When energy is lagging, decisions stall, or focus wanders
  • Slow down: During important character moments, major decisions, or emotional scenes
  • Add complexity: When players are succeeding too easily
  • Simplify: When players are overwhelmed or frustrated
## Compensation Principle Flowchart

When to Apply Compensation

  1. Has there been a streak of 3+ successes or failures?
  2. Is story momentum stalling?
  3. Is player engagement dropping?
  4. Are character development opportunities being missed?

If YES to any → Apply Compensation

How to Apply Compensation

After Success Streak

  1. Acknowledge successes
  2. Introduce future complications
  3. Create tension between successes
  4. Show unintended consequences
  5. Raise future stakes

After Failure Streak

  1. Honor mechanical failures
  2. Reveal new information
  3. Create unexpected advantages
  4. Introduce helpful elements
  5. Show character growth opportunities
## Dynamic Outcome Examples

Success (5-6) Examples

  • Combat: "Your attack lands perfectly. The guard falls, but his radio crackles with an incoming check-in request."
  • Social: "The merchant agrees to your terms enthusiastically—too enthusiastically. Word of this deal will spread quickly."
  • Exploration: "You find the hidden passage easily, but disturb ancient dust that begins to trigger forgotten wards."

Partial Success (3-4) Examples

  • Combat: "You strike the guard down, but not before he shouts an alarm."
  • Social: "The merchant agrees reluctantly, but demands payment in advance."
  • Exploration: "You find the hidden passage, but it's partially collapsed and will be difficult to navigate."

Setback (1-2) Examples

  • Combat: "Your attack misses, but knocks over a shelf that creates cover between you and the guard."
  • Social: "The merchant refuses your offer, but mentions another trader who might be more amenable."
  • Exploration: "You can't find the passage, but discover markings that suggest another entrance exists."
## Troubleshooting Common Issues

When Players Hesitate

  • Reference setting elements to provide context
  • Show clear asset opportunities
  • Connect to character goals
  • Present immediate stakes
  • Offer a default option

When Scenes Stall

  • Introduce setting complication
  • Bring in relationship connection
  • Escalate existing tension
  • Add time pressure
  • Change the scene location

When Choices Feel Meaningless

  • Show setting implications
  • Connect to character traits
  • Add asset considerations
  • Reveal hidden stakes
  • Create long-term consequences

When Players Struggle With Rules

  • Simplify the immediate choice
  • Offer a specific resolution method
  • Suggest asset or relationship use
  • Provide an example from previous play
  • Focus on intention over mechanics
## Session Management Tools

Scene Stagnation Checklist

If a scene is dragging, check for:

  • Clear character goals
  • Meaningful stakes
  • Multiple approach options
  • Setting integration
  • Time pressure or urgency

Player Engagement Assessment

Watch for signs of disengagement:

  • Distracted behavior
  • Reduced participation
  • Focusing on mechanics over story
  • Avoiding character moments
  • Deferring decisions to others

Narrative Momentum Tracker

Rate momentum from 1-5:

  1. Stalled (immediate intervention needed)
  2. Slow (needs new elements)
  3. Steady (maintaining interest)
  4. Building (increasing engagement)
  5. Climactic (peak intensity)

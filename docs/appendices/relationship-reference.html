Relationship Guide

Relationships are the connections between characters that provide both opportunities and complications. This guide explains how relationships work in Narrono and how to use them effectively in play.

Understanding Relationships

Each relationship has three key components:

  1. The Parties Involved: Who is connected (usually two characters, but can include groups)
  2. The Relationship Type: The nature of the connection (professional, personal, antagonistic, etc.)
  3. The Current State: How strong or strained the relationship is at present

Relationship Types

Relationships can take many forms, each with different implications:

Personal Relationships

  • Family Ties: Connections through blood or adoption
  • Friendships: Voluntary bonds based on mutual affection
  • Romantic Connections: Intimate emotional attachments
  • Mentorships: Guidance and learning relationships

Professional Relationships

  • Authority Chains: Hierarchical connections (boss/employee, officer/subordinate)
  • Collegial Bonds: Peer relationships in work or organizations
  • Client Connections: Service provider and recipient relationships
  • Alliance Partnerships: Formal cooperation between equals

Antagonistic Relationships

  • Rivalries: Competition for the same goals or resources
  • Blackmail Bonds: Relationships based on mutual leverage
  • Vendettas: Ongoing conflicts driven by past wrongs
  • Hunter/Hunted: Pursuit relationships

Community Relationships

  • Group Memberships: Belonging to organizations or communities
  • Public Personas: Relationships with broader society
  • Factional Ties: Connections to political or social movements
  • Cultural Bonds: Shared heritage or background connections

Relationship States

Like assets, relationships exist in different states that reflect their current condition:

Strong

  • Providing full benefits and support
  • Reliable and trustworthy
  • Adds one die to your pool when leveraged
  • Creates opportunities for both parties

Example: Elena's Strong relationship with the City Council gives her reliable influence over policy decisions.

Strained

  • Limited benefits with complications
  • Requires extra effort to maintain
  • No dice bonus when used
  • May create additional challenges

Example: Marcus's Strained blackmail bond with Elena means both must tread carefully, with trust broken down and subtle threats exchanged.

Broken

  • Temporarily unusable
  • Active hostility or complete disconnection
  • Provides no benefits and may create obstacles
  • Requires significant effort to repair

Example: If Clara's relationship with her captain becomes Broken, she might face disciplinary action and lose access to police resources.

Transformed

  • Changed into a different type of relationship
  • Fundamentally altered in nature
  • May be stronger or weaker than before
  • Creates new narrative possibilities

Example: A professional mentorship might transform into a friendship, or a rivalry might transform into a reluctant alliance.

Using Relationships in Play

Leveraging Strong Relationships

When a Strong relationship is relevant to your action:

  • Add one die to your pool
  • Describe how the relationship helps
  • Consider potential strain if the action fails
  • Create narrative connections to the other party

Managing Strained Relationships

When working with a Strained relationship:

  • Navigate carefully to avoid further damage
  • Look for opportunities to demonstrate good faith
  • Consider what would restore trust
  • Use the tension to create interesting story moments

Repairing Broken Relationships

To restore a Broken relationship:

  • Address the root cause of the break
  • Make meaningful amends or changes
  • Demonstrate commitment to improvement
  • Create a narrative moment of reconciliation

Transforming Relationships

Relationships can transform when:

  • Circumstances fundamentally change
  • A significant event affects both parties
  • The basis of the relationship shifts
  • Both parties agree to redefine their connection

Relationship Recovery

Minor Recovery (-1 Strain)

  • Meaningful conversation
  • Small gesture of good faith
  • Shared success in a minor challenge
  • Temporary alignment of goals

Major Recovery (-2 Strain)

  • Significant sacrifice for the other party
  • Resolving a long-standing issue
  • Shared triumph over a major obstacle
  • Profound moment of understanding

Complete Reset

  • Life-changing event that affects both parties
  • Fundamental transformation of circumstances
  • Extraordinary sacrifice or demonstration of trust
  • Mutual decision to begin anew

Relationship Examples in Play

Example 1: Leveraging a Strong Relationship

Situation: Clara needs information about a suspicious case file that's been restricted.

Relationship Use: She leverages her Strong relationship with Detective Sarah Chen.

Mechanics:

  • Base pool: 3d6
  • +1 die for Strong relationship
  • Roll 4d6, getting 1, 3, 4, 5
  • Using Direct Method (highest die): 5
  • Outcome: Success

Example 2: Navigating a Strained Relationship

Situation: Marcus needs to warn Elena about a threat to her family despite their Strained blackmail bond.

Mechanics:

  • Base pool: 3d6
  • No bonus die due to Strained relationship
  • Roll 3d6, getting 2, 3, 6
  • Using Cautious Method (lowest die): 2
  • Outcome: Setback

Narration: "Marcus approaches Elena after the council meeting, but his attempt at discretion comes across as suspicious. 'Whatever game you're playing, Webb, I'm not interested,' she says coldly, walking away before he can explain about the danger he's discovered."

Example 3: Repairing a Relationship

Situation: Clara's relationship with Captain Mercer has become Broken after he discovered her unauthorized investigation.

Recovery Action: Clara presents evidence that proves her suspicions were justified.

Narration: "Clara places the folder on Mercer's desk. 'I know I broke protocol, sir, but this is why.' As he reviews the evidence, his expression shifts from anger to concern. 'This doesn't excuse your methods, Hayes, but... you were right to dig deeper. Next time, come to me first.'"

Strategic Relationship Management

Building New Relationships

  • Identify potential valuable connections
  • Create opportunities for initial contact
  • Demonstrate value or common interest
  • Develop trust through consistent actions

Maintaining Multiple Relationships

  • Balance time and attention across connections
  • Be aware of conflicting loyalties
  • Look for synergies between relationships
  • Prioritize based on current needs and goals

Relationship Risk Assessment

Before risking strain on a relationship, consider:

  • How critical is the relationship to your goals?
  • Are there alternative relationships that could help?
  • What would recovery require?
  • Does the potential benefit outweigh the risk?

Relationship Evolution

As the story progresses, relationships naturally evolve:

  • New facets of connection emerge
  • Power dynamics shift
  • Trust deepens or erodes
  • Shared experiences create bonds or divisions

Remember that relationships are dynamic elements that respond to your choices and actions. Managing them effectively is as important as managing your assets or traits.

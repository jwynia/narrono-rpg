Character-to-Character Relationships Guide

This guide helps players and Game Facilitators (GFs) create meaningful relationships between player characters in Narrono, ensuring they have both narrative weight and mechanical significance.

1. Relationship Type Selection

Choose a relationship type that fits the setting and characters:

Family and Blood Ties

  • Siblings: Rivalry, responsibility, shared legacy
  • Distant Relatives: Rediscovered connections
  • Adopted Family: Chosen bonds
  • Legacy Inheritors: Shared burdens
  • Clan Representatives: Family duty

Professional Connections

  • Former Colleagues: Shared experience
  • Rivals in Same Field: Competition
  • Contractor and Client: Obligation
  • Superior and Subordinate: Authority
  • Partners with Unequal Status: Power imbalance

Debt and Obligation

  • Life-Debt: Extreme obligation
  • Financial Entanglement: Resources
  • Oath-Bound: Formal promise
  • Blackmail Relationship: Leverage
  • Inherited Obligation: Ancestral commitments

Shared History

  • Childhood Friends: Reconnection
  • Former Cell-mates: Prison bonds
  • Survivors of Disaster: Trauma bond
  • Fellow Veterans: Battlefield memories
  • Witnesses to Secret Event: Shared knowledge

Adversarial Connections

  • Former Enemies: Tentative alliance
  • Competing Heirs: Disputed inheritance
  • Ideological Opponents: Philosophical difference
  • Betrayer and Betrayed: Broken trust
  • Hunter and Quarry: Pursuit turned partnership

2. Status Establishment

Determine the current state of the relationship:

Strong

  • Full trust and cooperation
  • Reliable support
  • Clear communication
  • Shared goals or values
  • Provides full mechanical benefits

Strained

  • Underlying tensions
  • Recent disagreements
  • Conflicting priorities
  • Unresolved issues
  • Limited mechanical benefits

Broken

  • Active conflict
  • Betrayal or failure
  • Fundamental disagreement
  • Requires repair
  • No mechanical benefits until restored

Transformed

  • Recently changed relationship type
  • Evolving dynamics
  • New boundaries
  • Uncertain expectations
  • Modified mechanical benefits

3. Tension Point Identification

Identify 1-3 specific tensions that add depth and potential for development:

Internal Tensions

  • Conflicting Values: Different moral codes or priorities
  • Unequal Investment: One character more committed than the other
  • Unresolved Past: Lingering issues from shared history
  • Competing Needs: Both want different things from the relationship
  • Secret Resentment: Hidden feelings of anger or jealousy

External Tensions

  • Outside Pressures: Faction or family disapproval
  • Competing Loyalties: Obligations to others that conflict
  • Resource Scarcity: Not enough to satisfy both characters
  • Status Differences: Social or power imbalances
  • Third-Party Interference: Someone actively disrupting the bond

Setting-Based Tensions

  • Axis Conflicts: Different relationships to setting axes
  • Faction Tensions: Belonging to groups with opposing interests
  • Location Separation: Geographic challenges
  • Cultural Differences: Misunderstandings due to background
  • Historical Context: Setting history affecting the relationship

4. Development Potential

Plan how the relationship might evolve through play:

Strengthening Paths

  • Shared Adversity: Facing challenges together
  • Mutual Support: Helping each other achieve goals
  • Understanding Growth: Learning to accept differences
  • Trust Building: Proving reliability in critical moments
  • Boundary Respect: Honoring each other's limits

Complication Paths

  • Betrayal Risk: Situations that test loyalty
  • Competing Priorities: Forced to choose between relationship and other values
  • External Pressure: Others trying to drive characters apart
  • Secret Revelation: Hidden information coming to light
  • Resource Conflict: Forced to compete for limited assets

Transformation Paths

  • Role Reversal: Power dynamics shifting
  • Relationship Type Change: Evolving into a different connection
  • Mutual Growth: Both characters changing together
  • Independence Development: Becoming less codependent
  • Merging Goals: Finding common purpose

5. Integration with Traits

Connect the relationship to character traits for deeper integration:

Origin Connections

  • How do the characters' backgrounds interact?
  • Are there shared experiences or contrasting upbringings?
  • Do their origins create natural tensions or bonds?
  • How does social structure affect their connection?

Drive Interactions

  • Do their motivations complement or conflict?
  • Can one help the other achieve their drive?
  • How do their approaches to conflict differ or align?
  • What happens when drives pull in opposite directions?

Goal Alignment

  • Are their goals compatible or contradictory?
  • Can they help each other achieve their aims?
  • What happens if one succeeds while the other fails?
  • How might goals evolve through their relationship?

Asset Sharing

  • Which assets might they share?
  • How do their different assets complement each other?
  • What tensions might arise from asset imbalances?
  • How might assets be risked to support the relationship?

6. Mechanical Implementation

Apply concrete mechanical effects to the relationship:

Pool Support

  • Add relationship dice to pools when connection is relevant
  • Enable combined actions with enhanced effects
  • Allow connected characters to share certain assets
  • Provide recovery support for other relationships

Risk and Reward

  • Risk relationship status (strain) for additional dice
  • Gain favorable conditions when working together
  • Face complications when relationship tensions are triggered
  • Restore strained or broken relationships through narrative actions

Status Effects

  • Strong: Full dice benefits, asset sharing, favorable conditions
  • Strained: Reduced benefits, potential complications, limited sharing
  • Broken: No benefits, active complications, no sharing
  • Transformed: Modified benefits based on new relationship type

8. Example Relationships

Example 1: Former Rivals Now Allies

Character 1: Elara, Guild Mage
Character 2: Thorne, Rogue Spellcaster
Relationship Type: Former Rivals in Same Field
Current Status: Strained but Improving

Tension Points:
1. Elara still represents the guild that exiled Thorne
2. Both compete for rare magical resources
3. Thorne knows a secret about Elara's past

Trait Connections:
- Origin: Elara's privileged academy training vs. Thorne's self-taught methods
- Drive: Both seek magical knowledge but for different purposes
- Goal: Thorne wants recognition, Elara wants stability

Mechanical Effects:
- +1 die when combining magical approaches
- Complications arise when guild politics are involved
- Can share magical assets but with risk of strain

Development Notes:
- Could strengthen through facing a common magical threat
- Might transform into mentorship if one saves the other
- Risk of breaking if guild forces Elara to choose sides

Example 2: Siblings with Conflicting Loyalties

Character 1: Kira, Resistance Fighter
Character 2: Markus, Imperial Officer
Relationship Type: Siblings
Current Status: Broken

Tension Points:
1. Opposite sides of political conflict
2. Shared family secret that could destroy both
3. Competing claims to family inheritance

Trait Connections:
- Origin: Same family but chose different paths
- Drive: Both seek to honor parents' memory differently
- Goal: Kira wants revolution, Markus wants reform

Mechanical Effects:
- Currently no benefits due to broken status
- If restored, would provide strong social assets
- Each knows the other's weaknesses (tactical advantage)

Development Notes:
- Could be restored through family crisis requiring cooperation
- Might transform if one changes political allegiance
- Potential for powerful alliance if they find common ground

Notes for Game Facilitators

  • Use relationships as story drivers, not just mechanical benefits
  • Create scenes that specifically test or strengthen relationships
  • Allow relationships to evolve naturally through play
  • Encourage players to invoke relationships in creative ways
  • Balance relationship benefits with meaningful costs and risks
  • Use relationship tensions to create compelling complications
  • Reward players for playing into relationship dynamics
  • Document relationship changes as the campaign progresses

Use this guide to create rich, dynamic relationships between player characters that drive the narrative and provide meaningful mechanical interactions.

Character-to-Faction Relationships Guide

This guide helps players and Game Facilitators (GFs) create meaningful relationships between player characters and factions in Narrono, adding depth to the setting and creating narrative opportunities.

1. Position Establishment

Define the character's standing within or relationship to the faction:

Internal Positions

  • Core Member: Fully integrated, high commitment
  • Rising Member: Gaining influence, proving worth
  • Established Member: Stable position, known quantity
  • Marginal Member: On the periphery, limited involvement
  • Leadership: Decision-making authority

External Positions

  • Ally: Supportive but independent
  • Contractor: Professional relationship
  • Rival: Competitive but respectful
  • Adversary: Active opposition
  • Target: Pursued by the faction

Special Positions

  • Former Member: Left voluntarily or expelled
  • Infiltrator: Secret membership or spying
  • Debtor: Owes the faction
  • Patron: Supports the faction
  • Legacy Connection: Inherited relationship

2. Influence Mapping

Chart the bidirectional influence between character and faction:

Faction's Influence on Character

  • Resources: What assets the faction provides
  • Protection: How the faction shields the character
  • Limitations: Restrictions imposed by the relationship
  • Expectations: What the faction demands
  • Identity: How the connection shapes the character

Character's Influence on Faction

  • Contribution: What the character provides
  • Connections: Other relationships the character brings
  • Reputation: How the character affects faction standing
  • Direction: Character's impact on faction goals
  • Internal Politics: Allies and rivals within the faction

Influence Strength

  • Dominant: Overwhelming control or impact
  • Strong: Significant but not total influence
  • Moderate: Notable but limited influence
  • Weak: Minimal impact
  • Potential: Unrealized influence that could grow

3. Obligation Framework

Establish the commitments and expectations that define the relationship:

Formal Obligations

  • Contracts: Written agreements
  • Oaths: Sworn commitments
  • Debts: Resources owed
  • Positions: Official roles and duties
  • Membership Requirements: Standard expectations

Informal Obligations

  • Favors: Unwritten expectations
  • Loyalty: Expected allegiance
  • Representation: Behavior reflecting on faction
  • Support: Assistance in times of need
  • Information: Intelligence sharing

Obligation Balance

  • One-sided: Character owes much more (or less)
  • Imbalanced: Significant disparity in obligations
  • Balanced: Roughly equal commitments
  • Evolving: Shifting balance over time
  • Contested: Disagreement about obligations

4. Benefit Structure

Define what each party gains from the relationship:

Character Benefits

  • Material Resources: Equipment, funds, facilities
  • Information Access: Intelligence, knowledge, secrets
  • Status: Social standing, reputation
  • Protection: Security, backing in conflicts
  • Opportunities: Access to special situations

Faction Benefits

  • Skills/Abilities: Character's unique capabilities
  • Connections: Access to character's network
  • Representation: Character as public face
  • Deniability: Character for unofficial actions
  • Perspective: Character's unique viewpoint

Benefit Dynamics

  • Mutual Growth: Both sides prosper together
  • Zero-sum: One benefits at other's expense
  • Synergistic: Benefits multiply through cooperation
  • Diminishing Returns: Decreasing value over time
  • Potential: Unrealized benefits that could develop

5. Evolution Triggers

Identify events that could change the relationship:

Strengthening Triggers

  • Successful Collaboration: Achieving goals together
  • External Threat: Uniting against common enemy
  • Resource Exchange: Mutually beneficial sharing
  • Aligned Growth: Parallel development
  • Crisis Support: Standing together in difficulty

Straining Triggers

  • Failed Obligation: Not meeting expectations
  • Competing Interests: Goals coming into conflict
  • External Pressure: Outside forces creating tension
  • Resource Scarcity: Not enough to go around
  • Leadership Change: New direction for faction

Transformation Triggers

  • Major Victory/Defeat: Significant outcome changing dynamics
  • Character Evolution: Personal growth changing needs
  • Faction Evolution: Organizational change
  • Revelation: Discovery of new information
  • External Shift: Setting changes affecting relationship

6. Setting Integration

Connect the faction relationship to the Five Axes framework:

Power Scale

  • Personal: Individual relationships within faction
  • Local: Community-level faction influence
  • Regional: Extended reach and resources
  • Global: Widespread power and connections

Supernatural Level

  • None: Purely mundane organization
  • Hidden: Secret supernatural elements
  • Open: Known supernatural aspects
  • Dominant: Primarily supernatural in nature

Technology Level

  • Limited: Basic tools and resources
  • Moderate: Standard technology
  • Advanced: Cutting-edge capabilities
  • Fantastic: Beyond current possibilities

Social Structure

  • Tribal: Family/clan-based organization
  • Hierarchical: Clear chain of command
  • Merit-based: Advancement through achievement
  • Fluid: Adaptable and changing structure

Conflict Type

  • Direct: Physical/military approach
  • Social: Influence and reputation focus
  • Economic: Resource control emphasis
  • Ideological: Belief and value centered

7. Mechanical Implementation

Apply concrete mechanical effects to the relationship:

Asset Access

  • Faction-specific assets available to character
  • Requirements to access faction resources
  • Consequences of using faction assets
  • Asset recovery through faction connections

Pool Support

  • Faction backing providing dice in relevant situations
  • Reputation effects on social interactions
  • Knowledge access affecting information gathering
  • Resource access influencing preparation

Complications and Opportunities

  • Faction obligations creating story hooks
  • Rival factions creating complications
  • Advancement opportunities within faction
  • Faction conflicts pulling in character

Status Effects

  • Good Standing: Full benefits and support
  • Probationary: Limited benefits, proving loyalty
  • Tense: Reduced benefits, potential complications
  • Broken: No benefits, possible active hostility

9. Example Relationships

Example 1: Guild Member

Character: Elara, Artificer
Faction: Arcane Craftsmen Guild
Position: Rising Member
Current Standing: Good

Influence:
- Faction → Character: Strong resource access, moderate identity shaping
- Character → Faction: Weak overall, but growing reputation contribution

Obligations:
- Character owes: Quarterly dues, crafting contributions, adherence to guild code
- Faction provides: Training, market access, legal protection

Benefits:
- Character gains: Workshop access, material discounts, knowledge sharing
- Faction gains: Innovative techniques, quality products, fresh perspective

Evolution Triggers:
- Strengthening: Creating a remarkable invention, resolving guild dispute
- Straining: Missing dues, quality complaints, breaking guild secrets
- Transformation: Election to leadership, major discovery, rival guild offer

Setting Integration:
- Regional power scale (guild spans several cities)
- Open supernatural (magical crafting is known practice)
- Advanced technology (pushing boundaries of magical artifice)
- Hierarchical structure (clear ranks and advancement path)
- Economic conflict (competition with other guilds and markets)

Mechanical Effects:
- +1 die on crafting-related rolls when using guild resources
- Access to rare materials as assets
- Obligation to respond to guild summons (potential complication)

Example 2: Criminal Organization Target

Character: Thorne, Former Thief
Faction: The Obsidian Hand (criminal syndicate)
Position: Target (former member who left with valuable information)
Current Standing: Broken

Influence:
- Faction → Character: Strong threat, moderate limitation of movement
- Character → Faction: Weak but potential blackmail leverage

Obligations:
- Character owes: Return of stolen information, silence about operations
- Faction provides: Nothing currently (formerly protection and income)

Benefits:
- Character gains: Knowledge of syndicate operations (dangerous asset)
- Faction gains: Nothing currently (seeks to eliminate security risk)

Evolution Triggers:
- Strengthening: Unlikely, but possible through major leverage gain
- Straining: Further exposure of syndicate secrets, evading capture
- Transformation: Providing valuable service, blackmail success, leadership change

Setting Integration:
- Local power scale (syndicate controls one city district)
- Hidden supernatural (rumored deals with shadow entities)
- Moderate technology (standard security and weapons)
- Hierarchical structure (strict chain of command)
- Direct conflict (enforces through violence and intimidation)

Mechanical Effects:
- Complication: Syndicate agents may appear at inconvenient times
- Asset: Inside knowledge of criminal operations
- Potential ally if relationship can be transformed

Notes for Game Facilitators

  • Use faction relationships to create story hooks and complications
  • Allow characters to develop multiple faction relationships with varying levels of commitment
  • Create interesting tensions between different faction relationships
  • Use faction evolution to reflect the impact of character actions
  • Allow faction relationships to change based on character choices
  • Create scenes that specifically test or leverage faction relationships
  • Use factions to introduce new assets, information, and opportunities
  • Balance faction benefits with meaningful obligations

Use this guide to create rich, dynamic relationships between characters and factions that expand the setting and provide meaningful narrative and mechanical interactions.

Relationship Web Construction Guide

This guide helps players and Game Facilitators (GFs) create and manage the Relationship Web in Narrono, a network of connections between characters and factions that drives narrative and provides mechanical benefits.

1. Network Visualization

Create a visual representation of all relationships in the game:

Basic Visualization

  • Place character names in a circle or grid
  • Draw lines connecting related characters
  • Use different line styles for relationship types:
    • Solid lines for positive relationships
    • Dashed lines for strained relationships
    • Dotted lines for broken relationships
    • Double lines for transformed relationships
  • Add faction names outside the character circle
  • Connect characters to relevant factions

Enhanced Visualization

  • Use color coding for relationship types
  • Add symbols to represent tension points
  • Indicate relationship strength by line thickness
  • Show directional influence with arrows
  • Group related characters in clusters
  • Position factions based on their relationships to each other

Digital Tools

  • Mind mapping software
  • Relationship mapping tools
  • Simple drawing programs
  • Dedicated RPG relationship trackers
  • Collaborative online whiteboards

Physical Tools

  • Large paper or whiteboard
  • Index cards for characters and factions
  • Colored string or yarn for connections
  • Repositionable notes for changing elements
  • Tokens to mark tension points

2. Connection Density Guidelines

Determine the appropriate number and distribution of relationships:

Optimal Density

  • Minimum Connections: Each character should have at least 2-3 meaningful relationships
  • Maximum Connections: Avoid more than 5-7 connections per character
  • Group Size Formula: For a group of N characters, aim for N to 2N total relationships
  • Faction Connections: Each character should have 1-2 faction relationships

Connection Distribution

  • Even Distribution: Ensure all characters have similar numbers of connections
  • Varied Types: Mix relationship types (family, professional, adversarial, etc.)
  • Varied States: Include relationships in different states (strong, strained, broken)
  • Cross-Group Connections: Ensure no character is isolated from the group

Setting-Appropriate Density

  • Power Scale Impact:
    • Personal: More intimate, fewer connections
    • Global: More extensive, complex networks
  • Social Structure Impact:
    • Tribal: Dense family/clan connections
    • Fluid: More diverse, changing connections
  • Technology Level Impact:
    • Limited: Primarily local, direct connections
    • Advanced: More distant, mediated connections

Balance Considerations

  • Avoid one character becoming the "hub" of all relationships
  • Create triangular relationship structures for dynamic interactions
  • Ensure both allies and tensions exist in the network
  • Balance character-to-character and character-to-faction connections
  • Consider how relationships might evolve over time

3. Tension Distribution

Strategically place tensions throughout the Relationship Web:

Tension Types

  • Internal Character Tensions: Conflicting goals or values within a character
  • Interpersonal Tensions: Conflicts between characters
  • Character-Faction Tensions: Conflicts between characters and organizations
  • Faction-Faction Tensions: Conflicts between organizations
  • Setting Tensions: Conflicts arising from the world itself

Tension Placement

  • Distribute Across Web: Don't concentrate all tension in one area
  • Connect to Character Goals: Link tensions to what characters care about
  • Create Triangles: Three-way tensions create dynamic situations
  • Balance with Strong Bonds: Offset tensions with supportive relationships
  • Layer Tensions: Create both surface and deeper tensions

Tension Intensity

  • Low Intensity: Background tensions that color interactions
  • Medium Intensity: Active issues that require attention
  • High Intensity: Critical conflicts that demand resolution
  • Varied Intensity: Mix different levels for dynamic play
  • Escalating Potential: Tensions that could grow more serious

Tension Triggers

  • Identify Specific Events: What might activate each tension?
  • Create Warning Signs: How will players recognize rising tension?
  • Plan Consequences: What happens if tension boils over?
  • Design Resolution Paths: How might tensions be resolved?
  • Consider Chain Reactions: How might one tension affect others?

4. Balance Assessment

Evaluate and adjust the Relationship Web for engaging play:

Narrative Balance

  • Spotlight Distribution: Ensure all characters have meaningful relationships
  • Story Potential: Check that relationships create interesting situations
  • Agency Balance: Verify that all players can affect the web
  • Setting Integration: Confirm relationships reflect the game world
  • Development Paths: Ensure relationships can evolve meaningfully

Mechanical Balance

  • Resource Distribution: Check that relationship benefits are fairly distributed
  • Skill Complementarity: Ensure characters can support each other's weaknesses
  • Challenge Appropriateness: Verify that tensions create suitable difficulties
  • Recovery Options: Confirm that damaged relationships can be repaired
  • System Integration: Ensure relationships interact with other game mechanics

Group Dynamic Balance

  • Cooperation Potential: Verify that characters have reasons to work together
  • Conflict Potential: Ensure tensions create interesting but not destructive conflicts
  • Independence Balance: Check that characters maintain individual agency
  • Interdependence Balance: Ensure characters need each other
  • Player Comfort: Verify that relationship dynamics respect player boundaries

Assessment Questions

  1. Does each character have meaningful connections?
  2. Are there both strong bonds and interesting tensions?
  3. Do relationships create compelling story opportunities?
  4. Is mechanical benefit distributed fairly?
  5. Does the web reflect the setting appropriately?
  6. Can all relationships evolve in interesting ways?
  7. Are there sufficient connection points for new elements?
  8. Does the web create a cohesive group dynamic?

5. Evolution Planning

Prepare for how the Relationship Web will change through play:

Evolution Mechanisms

  • Session Triggers: Events during play that change relationships
  • Milestone Changes: Major shifts at significant story points
  • Player Initiation: Character-driven relationship changes
  • GF Initiation: Setting-driven relationship changes
  • System Mechanics: Game rules that modify relationships

Planning for Growth

  • New Connections: How and when new relationships might form
  • Strengthening Paths: How strained relationships might improve
  • Recovery Routes: How broken relationships might be restored
  • Transformation Opportunities: How relationships might change type
  • Faction Evolution: How organization relationships might develop

Tracking Changes

  • Visual Updates: Modifying the relationship map
  • Documentation: Recording relationship changes
  • History Tracking: Noting key events that changed relationships
  • State Tracking: Monitoring relationship states
  • Mechanical Updates: Adjusting game benefits accordingly

Narrative Integration

  • Relationship Scenes: Dedicated moments to explore connections
  • Consequence Reflection: Showing how relationship changes affect the story
  • Character Growth: Linking relationship evolution to character development
  • Setting Impact: Demonstrating how relationship changes affect the world
  • Theme Reinforcement: Using relationship evolution to highlight game themes

6. Implementation Process

Step-by-step approach to creating the Relationship Web:

Session Zero Process

  1. Create Characters Individually: Focus on setting connection first
  2. Share Character Concepts: Brief introduction of each character
  3. Brainstorm Connections: Players suggest potential relationships
  4. Establish Primary Relationships: Define 1-2 key connections per character
  5. Add Faction Relationships: Connect characters to organizations
  6. Identify Tensions: Add complexity to each relationship
  7. Visualize the Web: Create a map of all connections
  8. Balance Assessment: Evaluate and adjust as needed
  9. Document Details: Record specifics of each relationship
  10. Plan First Tests: Identify early scenes to explore relationships

Mid-Campaign Process

  1. Review Current Web: Assess the state of all relationships
  2. Document Changes: Update based on play events
  3. Identify Stagnant Areas: Find relationships needing attention
  4. Plan Development Scenes: Create opportunities for evolution
  5. Introduce New Elements: Add new connections as appropriate
  6. Resolve or Escalate Tensions: Move conflicts forward
  7. Rebalance if Needed: Adjust to maintain engaging dynamics
  8. Update Visualization: Revise the relationship map
  9. Check Mechanical Effects: Ensure system integration
  10. Plan Next Phase: Identify future evolution opportunities

Introduction

Welcome to Narrono, a tabletop roleplaying game where you and your friends collaboratively build a dramatic story that unfolds through play. In this game, your choices—both before the game begins and during each moment of play—shape the narrative in profound and exciting ways.

What is Narrono?

At its heart, Narrono is a game about collaborative storytelling. Players and Game Facilitator work together to create compelling narratives where:

  • You shape the story through meaningful choices that have lasting impact
  • Your creative vision matters as much as the Game Facilitator's
  • The narrative belongs to everyone at the table
  • Drama emerges organically from character choices and their consequences
  • The mechanics serve the story, not the other way around

Narrono is a medium-complexity roleplaying system designed to create stories where:

  • Every action and choice creates rippling consequences throughout the narrative
  • Settings are defined by clear choices that shape the world and its possibilities
  • Characters emerge from and influence their settings
  • Characters are defined by narrative elements that both enable and complicate their actions
  • Assets can be risked for greater effect, creating story opportunities
  • Relationships bind characters together in setting-appropriate ways

Whether you're exploring a tribal society with limited technology and direct conflicts, or navigating a fluid social structure with fantastic technology and ideological struggles, Narrono provides the framework for meaningful character choices and evolving narratives where your decisions truly matter.

A Game of Collaborative Storytelling

In Narrono, the story isn't predetermined—it's created moment by moment through the choices you make. The Game Facilitator presents situations and challenges, but how those situations unfold depends entirely on what your characters choose to do and how they choose to do it.

Your choices begin even before play starts. During setting creation, you'll help determine what kind of world your story takes place in. During character creation, you'll define who your character is and how they connect to that world. And during play, every decision you make—from which traits to emphasize to which relationships to risk—shapes the unfolding narrative in ways neither you nor the Game Facilitator could have predicted.

This collaborative approach means that everyone at the table has genuine ownership of the story. The Game Facilitator may guide the overall direction, but the specific path, the emotional beats, and the ultimate resolution emerge from the collective creativity of the entire group.

Core Concepts

Before diving into the rules, here are the key concepts that make Narrono unique:

The Five Axes

Every setting in Narrono is defined by five axes, each representing a fundamental aspect of the world:

  • Power Scale: From Personal to Global
  • Supernatural Level: From None to Dominant
  • Technology Level: From Limited to Fantastic
  • Social Structure: From Tribal to Fluid
  • Conflict Type: From Direct to Ideological

These axes don't just describe your setting—they actively shape what characters can do, what assets are available, and how relationships function.

Character Traits

Characters in Narrono are defined by three core traits that emerge directly from the setting:

  • Origin: Shaped by the Social Structure
  • Drive: Shaped by the Conflict Type
  • Goal: Shaped by the Power Scale

Each trait not only tells you who your character is but also creates bonds, complications, and weaknesses that drive the story forward.

The Pool System

Actions in Narrono are resolved using the Pool System—a flexible dice mechanic that balances narrative possibility with strategic choice:

  • Roll three six-sided dice (3d6)
  • Choose which die to use based on your method (Direct, Balanced, or Cautious)
  • Interpret the result as Success, Partial Success, or Setback
  • Narrate the outcome and its consequences

Assets and Relationships

Your character's equipment, abilities, and connections are represented by:

  • Assets: Resources that can be used and risked
  • Relationships: Connections to other characters that provide support and complications

Both assets and relationships exist in multiple states (Fresh/Strong, Degraded/Strained, Depleted/Broken) that reflect their condition and availability.

The Shared Story Pool

The Shared Story Pool represents the group's collective influence on the narrative:

  • Each player contributes dice to a shared pool
  • Players can take dice to add complexity, establish relationships, create opportunities, or enhance rolls
  • This allows everyone to contribute to the story, even when their characters aren't directly involved

Let's Begin

The heart of Narrono lies in creating characters deeply connected to their setting and to each other. As you read through these rules, remember that every mechanical choice is also a narrative one. Your character's traits, assets, and relationships aren't just numbers on a sheet—they're the threads that weave your character into the story.

Chapter 1: Core Rules

The Pool System

At the heart of Narrono lies the Pool System - a flexible dice mechanic that balances narrative possibility with strategic choice. Every significant action your character takes is resolved through this system, creating a framework where your character's traits, assets, and relationships all influence the outcome.

Building Your Pool

When your character attempts a significant action, you'll build a dice pool following these steps:

  1. Start with three six-sided dice (3d6) - these represent your base capabilities
  2. Consider which of your character's traits (Origin, Drive, or Goal) best applies to the action
  3. Decide which resolution method you'll use based on trait alignment
  4. Determine if any assets or relationships can support your action
  5. Roll all dice in your pool

Example: Building a Pool

Kira, a Mountain Clan Warrior, is attempting to intimidate a merchant into revealing information. Her player considers:

  • Her Origin trait (Mountain Clan Warrior) strongly applies to intimidation
  • She has a Fresh "Fearsome Reputation" asset that could help
  • She decides to use the Direct method since her trait strongly aligns with the action

She rolls her 3d6 pool, getting 2, 4, and 6.

Resolution Methods

The Pool System offers three distinct ways to resolve actions, each representing a different approach to challenges. Your choice of method reflects how your character's traits align with the current action.

Direct Method

When to use: Your character's traits strongly support the action
How it works: Use the highest die in your pool
Narrative meaning: Acting with confidence and natural ability
Strategic consideration: Higher chance of success but also higher risk

Example: Direct Method

Kira uses the Direct method for intimidation since it aligns with her Mountain Clan Warrior trait. From her roll of 2, 4, and 6, she uses the highest die (6), achieving a Success. The merchant nervously reveals what he knows, but word of her intimidation tactics will spread.

Balanced Method

When to use: No traits strongly apply to the action
How it works: Use the middle die in your pool
Narrative meaning: Taking a measured, careful approach
Strategic consideration: Most consistent results with moderate impact

Example: Balanced Method

Later, Kira needs to negotiate a fair price. No trait strongly applies, so she uses the Balanced method. From a roll of 1, 3, and 5, she uses the middle die (3), achieving a Partial Success. She gets a fair price but must offer something in return.

Cautious Method

When to use: Acting against your character's trait tendencies
How it works: Use the lowest die in your pool
Narrative meaning: Carefully handling an unfamiliar situation
Strategic consideration: Lower chance of success but reduced risk

Example: Cautious Method

When Kira must navigate noble court etiquette, she uses the Cautious method since this goes against her direct Mountain Clan ways. From a roll of 2, 3, and 6, she uses the lowest die (2), resulting in a Setback. She makes a social blunder, but learns valuable information about court dynamics in the process.

Understanding Outcomes

Every action in Narrono results in one of three outcome levels, each with distinct narrative implications:

Success (5-6)

A Success means your character achieves their intention and strengthens their position. You get what you want and gain an additional benefit:

  • Achieve your stated intention
  • Strengthen your position in some way
  • Add a beneficial story element
  • Potentially improve an asset or relationship condition

Example: Success Outcome

Kira's intimidation Success (6) means the merchant not only reveals the information she seeks but also offers additional details to stay on her good side. Her reputation for getting results spreads, potentially helping in future interactions.

Partial Success (3-4)

A Partial Success means your character achieves their intention but at a cost or with a complication:

  • Achieve your stated intention
  • Face a minor complication or cost
  • Create a mixed opportunity
  • Potentially stress assets or relationships

Example: Partial Success Outcome

Kira's negotiation Partial Success (3) means she gets the fair price she wanted, but must offer the merchant priority on future goods from her clan. This creates both an opportunity (established trade relationship) and a complication (obligation).

Setback (1-2)

A Setback means your character misses their intention and faces a significant complication:

  • Miss your stated intention
  • Face a major complication
  • Assets or relationships may degrade
  • Create significant story impact

Example: Setback Outcome

Kira's court etiquette Setback (2) means she not only fails to make a good impression but accidentally insults an important noble. However, another outsider at court notices her struggle and may become a potential ally.

Asset Integration

Assets represent your character's equipment, special abilities, and resources. They exist in three states that reflect their condition and availability:

Asset States

  • Fresh: Providing full benefits and support
  • Degraded: Creating complications but still usable
  • Depleted: Temporarily unusable until restored

Using Assets

Assets can support your actions in several ways:

  1. Support: A Fresh asset can add a die to your pool or provide favorable conditions
  2. Risk: You can risk degrading an asset to reroll a die
  3. Recovery: Assets can be restored through narrative actions and story opportunities

Example: Asset Use

Kira uses her Fresh "Fearsome Reputation" asset when intimidating the merchant, adding a favorable condition to her roll. Later, when negotiating, she risks this asset by leaning too heavily on her reputation, causing it to become Degraded as locals begin to see her as a bully rather than a respected warrior.

The Relationship Web

Relationships in Narrono are more than narrative connections - they're mechanical elements that influence actions and create story opportunities. The Relationship Web connects characters to each other in setting-appropriate ways.

Relationship States

Like assets, relationships exist in multiple states:

  • Strong: Provides full benefits and support
  • Strained: Creates complications but still provides some benefits
  • Broken: Temporarily unusable until restored
  • Transformed: Changed into a new type of relationship

Using Relationships

Relationships can be used in several ways:

  1. Support: A Strong relationship can add a die to your pool or provide favorable conditions
  2. Combined Actions: Connected characters can work together for enhanced effects
  3. Asset Sharing: Relationships enable characters to share certain assets
  4. Risk: You can risk straining a relationship for greater effect

Example: Relationship Use

Kira has a Strong relationship with Doran, a Merchant Guild Apprentice (former enemies turned allies). When negotiating with the merchant, she can invoke this relationship for support, adding a favorable condition to her roll. However, if she pushes too hard, this relationship could become Strained as Doran's guild connections are compromised.

Relationship Types

Relationships fall into several categories based on the setting's axes:

  • Family and Blood Ties: Siblings, distant relatives, adopted family
  • Professional Connections: Colleagues, rivals, partners
  • Debt and Obligation: Life-debts, financial entanglements, oaths
  • Shared History: Childhood friends, survivors, witnesses
  • Adversarial Connections: Former enemies, competing heirs, ideological opponents

Example: Relationship Types

Kira and Doran have an "Adversarial Connection" that has evolved into an "Uneasy Alliance." Their relationship began as "Former Enemies" but transformed through shared experiences. This history creates both opportunities (unique perspectives) and complications (lingering distrust).

The Shared Story Pool

The Shared Story Pool represents the group's collective influence on the narrative. At the start of each session:

  • Each player adds one d6
  • The GM adds two d6s
  • Place these dice where all can reach them

Using the Shared Story Pool

Players can take dice from the pool to:

  1. Add Complexity: Introduce a complication connected to setting elements
  2. Establish Relationships: Create a connection that fits setting parameters
  3. Create Opportunities: Add a story element that opens new possibilities
  4. Enhance Rolls: Add a die to your personal pool with narrative justification

Example: Shared Story Pool

During the negotiation scene, another player takes a die from the Shared Story Pool to reveal that the merchant has ties to Kira's rivals, adding complexity to the situation. Later, Kira takes a die to establish that she once saved the merchant's daughter, creating a new relationship that could help in future interactions.

Putting It All Together

The true power of Narrono's system emerges when these elements work together. Consider this integrated example:

Integrated Example: The Merchant Negotiation

Situation: Kira needs to negotiate for supplies while hiding her true purpose.

Trait Consideration: Her Origin (Mountain Clan Warrior) doesn't strongly apply, suggesting a Balanced approach.

Asset Integration: Her "Trade Routes Knowledge" asset is Fresh and can provide support.

Relationship Use: Her Strong relationship with Doran gives her insight into guild practices.

Method Choice: She chooses the Balanced method due to mixed trait relevance.

Pool Building:

  • Base pool: 3d6
  • Fresh asset provides favorable condition
  • Relationship with Doran provides additional insight

Roll Result: 2, 4, 5

Outcome: Using the middle die (4), Kira achieves a Partial Success.

Narrative Resolution: Kira secures the supplies at a fair price (achieving intention) but must reveal part of her journey plans to the merchant (complication). Her knowledge of trade routes (asset) helps identify the best deals, while Doran's advice (relationship) helps her navigate guild customs. The merchant agrees to keep her secret but will expect a favor in return.

Story Development: Another player takes a die from the Shared Story Pool to establish that the merchant has connections to a faction Kira will encounter later, creating a future story opportunity.

Strategic Considerations

When to Use Direct Method

  • Your traits strongly support the action
  • You need a high probability of full success
  • You can handle potential complications
  • The narrative impact of failure could be interesting
  • Asset or relationship support can mitigate the risk

When to Use Balanced Method

  • Multiple traits have mixed relevance
  • Consistency is more important than peak success
  • You want to preserve assets and relationships
  • The situation requires careful navigation
  • You need to maintain multiple options

When to Use Cautious Method

  • Acting against trait tendencies
  • Failure would be catastrophic
  • Assets or relationships are already strained
  • You need to preserve resources
  • The situation requires subtle action

Risk Assessment

Consider these factors when risking assets or relationships:

  1. Current State

    • Fresh/Strong elements offer flexibility
    • Degraded/Strained indicate prior strain
    • Depleted/Broken need recovery focus

  2. Recovery Opportunities

    • Known restoration methods
    • Upcoming story possibilities
    • Group resource availability
    • Relationship support options

  3. Strategic Value

    • Short-term utility
    • Long-term importance
    • Narrative significance
    • Connection implications

Special Circumstances

Favorable Conditions

When circumstances clearly advantage you:

  • Add one die to pool
  • Drop lowest before applying method
  • Describe advantage in setting terms
  • May involve relationship benefits

Unfavorable Conditions

When circumstances clearly hinder you:

  • Remove one die from pool
  • Must use remaining dice
  • Describe hindrance in setting terms
  • May involve relationship complications

Asset/Relationship Support

When using appropriate support:

  • Add one die to pool
  • Element must be Fresh/Strong
  • Describe how it helps
  • Create narrative connection

Asset/Relationship Risk

When willing to risk degradation:

  • Can reroll any one die
  • Element becomes Degraded/Strained
  • Describe strain on element
  • Consider recovery options

Key Principles to Remember

  1. Narrative Drives Mechanics: Your character's traits, assets, and relationships should inform your mechanical choices.

  2. Methods Match Approach: Choose your resolution method based on how your character would naturally approach the situation.

  3. Outcomes Create Story: Every success, partial success, and setback should move the story forward in interesting ways.

  4. Risk Creates Opportunity: Risking assets and relationships creates both mechanical advantages and narrative possibilities.

  5. Connections Matter: The Relationship Web provides both support and complications, reflecting how characters influence each other.

  6. Setting Shapes Options: The five axes of your setting influence which traits, assets, and relationships are available and how they function.

  7. Group Storytelling: The Shared Story Pool allows all players to contribute to the narrative, even when their characters aren't directly involved.

By understanding these core mechanics, you'll be ready to create characters that are deeply connected to their setting and to each other, facing challenges that test their abilities and relationships in meaningful ways.

Chapter 2: Setting Creation

The Foundation of Your Story

In Narrono, your setting isn't just a backdrop—it's the foundation from which characters, conflicts, and stories emerge. The choices you make about your world directly shape what traits are available to characters, what assets make sense, and what kinds of relationships form between them.

This chapter guides you through creating a setting using the Five Axes framework—a system that helps you make clear, meaningful choices about your world's fundamental aspects. By the end, you'll have a rich, coherent setting that generates interesting character options and story possibilities.

The Five Axes Framework

The Five Axes framework helps create unique settings by making clear choices about fundamental aspects of the world. Each axis presents distinct options that combine to create different possibilities. These aren't sliding scales—each choice brings certain elements into focus while pushing others to the background.

Power Scale

The Power Scale axis determines the scope of meaningful action in your setting—what level of influence can characters realistically achieve?

Personal

  • Core Concept: Conflicts affect individuals and small groups
  • Character Impact: Success means changing immediate circumstances
  • Relationship Focus: Intimate and direct connections
  • Asset Types: Personal items, close bonds, individual skills
  • Story Implications: Narratives center on personal growth and local impact

Example: Personal Power Scale

In "Mountain Valley," a remote settlement cut off from wider civilization, conflicts revolve around family disputes, personal rivalries, and immediate survival challenges. Characters might become respected elders or skilled hunters, but their influence rarely extends beyond the valley itself. Relationships focus on direct bonds between individuals, and assets tend to be personal tools, shelters, and reputation among valley residents.

Local

  • Core Concept: Conflicts affect neighborhoods or communities
  • Character Impact: Success means influencing local dynamics
  • Relationship Focus: Community networks and organizations
  • Asset Types: Community resources, local reputation, group affiliations
  • Story Implications: Narratives involve community change and development

Example: Local Power Scale

In "Harbor Town," a bustling port city, characters can influence neighborhood politics, guild operations, or local trade. Success might mean becoming a guild master or neighborhood leader. Relationships span community networks like merchant associations or neighborhood watches. Assets include workshop ownership, crew loyalty, or standing in the harbor council.

Regional

  • Core Concept: Conflicts affect cities or nations
  • Character Impact: Success means shaping institutional policy
  • Relationship Focus: Organizational ties and political alliances
  • Asset Types: Significant resources, authority positions, influential networks
  • Story Implications: Narratives involve political maneuvering and large-scale change

Example: Regional Power Scale

In "The Seven Duchies," characters navigate the politics of rival noble houses and competing cities. Success means becoming a duke, high priest, or merchant prince with influence across multiple cities. Relationships include political alliances, trade partnerships, and institutional hierarchies. Assets represent major holdings, military commands, or religious authority.

Global

  • Core Concept: Conflicts affect the entire world
  • Character Impact: Success means changing history
  • Relationship Focus: International networks and world-spanning organizations
  • Asset Types: Major power structures, world-changing resources
  • Story Implications: Narratives involve world-altering events and historical shifts

Example: Global Power Scale

In "Nexus Earth," characters operate within international organizations that shape world events. Success means becoming a corporate CEO, nation leader, or head of a global movement. Relationships span international networks like diplomatic corps or multinational syndicates. Assets include control of rare resources, command of military forces, or leadership of global movements.

Supernatural Level

The Supernatural Level axis defines how the impossible interacts with the possible—how much does magic or the supernatural influence your world?

None

  • Core Concept: No supernatural elements exist
  • Character Impact: Conflicts stem from human nature and natural forces
  • Knowledge Systems: Understanding comes through observation and reason
  • Asset Types: Purely mundane tools, skills, and resources
  • Story Implications: Narratives focus on human drama and realistic challenges

Example: No Supernatural

In "Steel & Strategy," a historical setting based on Renaissance Italy, all events have natural explanations. Characters face purely human challenges—political intrigue, economic competition, and military conflict. Knowledge comes through study, observation, and experience. Assets are realistic tools, political positions, and social connections.

Hidden

  • Core Concept: Supernatural exists in shadows and secret places
  • Character Impact: Conflicts involve secrecy, revelation, and hidden power
  • Knowledge Systems: Occult knowledge is dangerous and protected
  • Asset Types: Secret lore, subtle supernatural tools, hidden sanctums
  • Story Implications: Narratives involve discovery, conspiracy, and hidden worlds

Example: Hidden Supernatural

In "Veiled Streets," supernatural forces operate beneath the surface of a modern city. Secret societies of practitioners guard ancient knowledge, while ordinary people remain unaware of the hidden battles around them. Knowledge of the supernatural is carefully guarded, passed through initiation or discovered through dangerous investigation. Assets might include subtle charms, secret hideouts, or forbidden texts.

Open

  • Core Concept: Supernatural is known but uncommon
  • Character Impact: Conflicts mix mundane and magical elements
  • Knowledge Systems: Supernatural knowledge is regulated or controlled
  • Asset Types: Licensed magical items, registered supernatural abilities
  • Story Implications: Narratives explore the integration of the extraordinary into ordinary life

Example: Open Supernatural

In "Arcane Republic," magic is a recognized but regulated force. Licensed practitioners serve in specialized roles, while magical creatures are classified and monitored. Knowledge of magic is taught in academies under government oversight. Assets include official magical licenses, registered artifacts, and certified familiar bonds.

Dominant

  • Core Concept: Supernatural forces shape society fundamentally
  • Character Impact: Conflicts center on supernatural forces and abilities
  • Knowledge Systems: Understanding the supernatural is crucial for daily life
  • Asset Types: Powerful magical tools, supernatural bonds, magical territories
  • Story Implications: Narratives explore the implications of a world defined by supernatural forces

Example: Dominant Supernatural

In "Elemental Domains," society is organized around magical affinities, with cities built from living elements and transportation achieved through dimensional folding. Understanding magical principles is as basic as literacy. Assets include elemental bonds, reality-shaping tools, and connections to magical lineages.

Technology Level

The Technology Level axis establishes the tools and innovations available in your setting—what can people build and how does it shape their lives?

Limited

  • Core Concept: Pre-industrial technology
  • Character Impact: Conflicts emphasize personal skill and direct action
  • Information Flow: Knowledge travels slowly through oral tradition and manuscripts
  • Asset Types: Handcrafted tools, rare materials, inherited items
  • Story Implications: Narratives emphasize personal capability and resource scarcity

Example: Limited Technology

In "Coastal Kingdoms," technology resembles medieval Europe. Travel is by horse or sail, communication depends on messengers, and production relies on manual craftsmanship. Information travels slowly through manuscripts and oral tradition. Assets include well-crafted weapons, sturdy ships, and productive land.

Moderate

  • Core Concept: Industrial age capability
  • Character Impact: Conflicts balance traditional methods with emerging technologies
  • Information Flow: Printed materials and early telecommunications spread knowledge
  • Asset Types: Mass-produced goods, early industrial machinery, specialized equipment
  • Story Implications: Narratives explore changing societies and disrupted traditions

Example: Moderate Technology

In "Steam & Smoke," technology resembles the late 19th century. Railways connect cities, telegraphs transmit messages, and factories produce standardized goods. Information spreads through newspapers, telegrams, and mass-printed books. Assets include steam-powered vehicles, precision firearms, and industrial facilities.

Advanced

  • Core Concept: Modern or near-future tech
  • Character Impact: Conflicts leverage technology for advantage
  • Information Flow: Digital networks enable instant global communication
  • Asset Types: Computerized systems, advanced vehicles, digital resources
  • Story Implications: Narratives explore the impact of technology on human connection

Example: Advanced Technology

In "Network Society," technology resembles our current world with some advancements. Smart devices connect everyone, automation handles routine tasks, and information flows instantly through global networks. Assets include cutting-edge devices, proprietary algorithms, and digital influence.

Fantastic

  • Core Concept: Beyond current scientific understanding
  • Character Impact: Conflicts shaped by technological innovation and control
  • Information Flow: Information is instantaneous and ubiquitous
  • Asset Types: Reality-altering devices, consciousness technology, matter manipulation
  • Story Implications: Narratives question the nature of reality and humanity

Example: Fantastic Technology

In "Quantum Horizons," technology has transcended current limitations. Matter can be restructured at will, consciousness can be transferred between bodies, and reality itself can be locally modified. Information exists in a collective dataspace accessible by thought. Assets include reality shaping devices, consciousness backups, and quantum entanglement networks.

Social Structure

The Social Structure axis defines how people organize and relate to each other—how is society structured and how do people gain status?

Tribal

  • Core Concept: Society based on family and clan connections
  • Character Impact: Conflicts involve group loyalty and tradition
  • Status System: Position comes from tradition and lineage
  • Asset Types: Heritage items, clan territories, traditional knowledge
  • Story Implications: Narratives explore tradition, belonging, and group identity

Example: Tribal Structure

In "Nomad Clans," society organizes around extended family groups with strong traditions. Leadership passes through bloodlines or adoption into the clan. Status comes from upholding traditions and contributing to clan welfare. Assets include ancestral weapons, clan territories, and traditional knowledge passed through generations.

Hierarchical

  • Core Concept: Rigid class divisions with clear social levels
  • Character Impact: Conflicts span social levels and class boundaries
  • Status System: Position is primarily inherited or assigned at birth
  • Asset Types: Class markers, hereditary privileges, formal titles
  • Story Implications: Narratives explore class tension and social mobility challenges

Example: Hierarchical Structure

In "Imperial Domains," society divides into nobles, clergy, merchants, and commoners with strict rules governing interactions. Birth largely determines one's place, with limited mobility through marriage, exceptional service, or rare talent. Assets include noble titles, official positions, and class-specific privileges.

Merit-based

  • Core Concept: Achievement determines social position
  • Character Impact: Conflicts involve proving worth and demonstrating capability
  • Status System: Position must be earned through demonstrated ability
  • Asset Types: Credentials, proven achievements, recognized innovations
  • Story Implications: Narratives explore ambition, competition, and the meaning of merit

Example: Merit-based Structure

In "The Meritocracy," formal testing and demonstrated achievement determine social position. Leadership roles require specific qualifications and ongoing performance evaluation. Status comes from recognized accomplishments and certified skills. Assets include prestigious credentials, performance records, and innovation patents.

Fluid

  • Core Concept: Constantly changing social dynamics
  • Character Impact: Conflicts reshape social connections and groupings
  • Status System: Position is temporary and contextual
  • Asset Types: Adaptable resources, flexible networks, transferable skills
  • Story Implications: Narratives explore adaptation, reinvention, and impermanence

Example: Fluid Structure

In "Shift Society," social groupings form and dissolve based on immediate needs and opportunities. Leadership emerges contextually rather than through fixed positions. Status fluctuates based on current relevance and contribution. Assets include adaptable skills, flexible resources, and the ability to navigate changing social landscapes.

Conflict Type

The Conflict Type axis establishes how problems are typically solved in your setting—what approaches do people use to resolve their differences?

Direct

  • Core Concept: Physical or military solutions predominate
  • Character Impact: Conflicts resolved through force or its threat
  • Consequence Pattern: Immediate and tangible results
  • Asset Types: Weapons, defensive capabilities, physical resources
  • Story Implications: Narratives feature clear confrontations and decisive actions

Example: Direct Conflict

In "Frontier Territories," disputes are commonly settled through physical confrontation or shows of force. Military strength determines regional control, and personal combat skills are highly valued. Consequences are immediate and visible—territory gained or lost, resources secured or surrendered. Assets include weapons, fortifications, and fighting forces.

Social

  • Core Concept: Status and influence determine outcomes
  • Character Impact: Conflicts require relationship navigation and reputation management
  • Consequence Pattern: Reputation has lasting impact beyond immediate situation
  • Asset Types: Social connections, status symbols, reputation capital
  • Story Implications: Narratives explore complex relationship webs and indirect influence

Example: Social Conflict

Setting Evolution

Settings in Narrono aren't static—they evolve through play as characters affect their world. Understanding how settings change helps you create dynamic, responsive worlds.

Understanding Change

Changes happen at three levels:

1. Element Changes

These are shifts within the current axis positions:

  • New factions emerge within existing structures
  • Relationships between groups shift
  • Resources become available or scarce
  • Knowledge spreads or becomes restricted

Example: Element Changes in Shadowtide Harbor

  • A new faction forms within the Harbor Council
  • The relationship between the Mechanics Guild and Twilight Circle shifts from hostile to cooperative
  • A new vein of shadowcoal is discovered
  • Knowledge of a specific supernatural ability becomes more widespread

2. Internal Axis Shifts

These are changes within an axis choice that don't jump to a new category:

  • Power structures reorganize within their scale
  • Supernatural forces find new expressions
  • Technology develops within its level
  • Social structures adapt while maintaining their nature
  • Conflict patterns evolve within their type

Example: Internal Shifts in Shadowtide Harbor

  • The Harbor Council reforms its leadership structure (within Hierarchical)
  • New types of supernatural abilities emerge (within Hidden)
  • Advanced steam technology develops (within Moderate)
  • Social influence tactics evolve (within Social Conflict)

3. Axis Transitions

These are major changes that move from one axis choice to another:

  • Require significant story events
  • Should feel earned through play
  • Create lasting consequences
  • Usually happen gradually

Example: Potential Axis Transition in Shadowtide Harbor

After many sessions of play, the characters' actions lead to supernatural abilities becoming publicly known and acknowledged, shifting the Supernatural Level from Hidden to Open. This creates cascading effects across the setting:

  • New regulations for supernatural abilities
  • Public institutions for supernatural training
  • Changed social dynamics as abilities become status markers
  • Evolved conflict patterns as supernatural elements enter public discourse

Managing Evolution

Gradual Change

Most evolution should happen slowly:

  1. Establish current state clearly
  2. Show signs of potential change
  3. Create opportunities for character influence
  4. Let consequences ripple outward

Example: Gradual Change

The transition from Hidden to Open Supernatural might follow this progression:

  1. Rumors of strange abilities begin to circulate
  2. Public incidents become harder to cover up
  3. Characters have opportunities to reveal or conceal supernatural events
  4. Based on their choices, public awareness grows or is suppressed
  5. Eventually, a tipping point is reached where denial is no longer possible

Sudden Shifts

Sometimes dramatic events force rapid change:

  • Should be rare and significant
  • Must emerge from character actions
  • Create immediate problems
  • Require adaptation

Example: Sudden Shift

A catastrophic event at the harbor—perhaps a massive supernatural manifestation during a public ceremony—could force an immediate shift from Hidden to Open Supernatural, creating:

  • Immediate panic and confusion
  • Rapid policy responses
  • Social upheaval
  • New threats and opportunities

Advanced Resolution Mechanics

The Pool System Revisited

At its core, Narrono uses the Pool System for resolving significant actions:

  1. Form a pool of three six-sided dice (3d6)
  2. Roll all dice in your pool
  3. Choose which result to use based on your method (Direct, Balanced, or Cautious)
  4. Apply any asset or relationship effects
  5. Determine the outcome level (Success, Partial Success, or Setback)

While this basic framework remains consistent, the strategic depth comes from understanding when and how to use different methods, how to leverage assets and relationships, and how to interpret outcomes in narratively satisfying ways.

Strategic Method Selection

Your choice of resolution method—Direct, Balanced, or Cautious—should be a strategic decision based on your character's traits, the situation, and your narrative goals.

Direct Method (Highest Die)

The Direct method represents confident, straightforward action aligned with your character's traits. When you choose Direct, you're taking decisive action in an area where your character excels.

When to Use Direct:

  • Your traits strongly support the action
  • You need a high probability of full success
  • You can handle potential complications
  • The narrative impact of failure could be interesting
  • Asset or relationship support can mitigate the risk

Strategic Considerations:

  • Direct offers the highest chance of Success (55.6%)
  • But also has a significant chance of Setback (25.9%)
  • Creates stronger narrative impact in both success and failure
  • Best used when you've positioned your assets and relationships to handle potential setbacks

Example: Direct Method in Action

Mira needs to extract information from a rival noble at a court gathering. She chooses the Direct method because:

  • Her Origin as "Third Tier Noble of House Vayne" aligns perfectly with court interaction
  • She has her "Network of Informants" asset Fresh and ready to provide support
  • Her relationship with Elara is Strong and can help if complications arise
  • Even a Setback would create interesting narrative possibilities

The GF describes how Mira skillfully navigates the conversation, using her noble upbringing and subtle hints about information she already knows to extract the secrets she seeks. Her success strengthens her position in court but also creates a future complication as the rival begins to suspect her motives.

Balanced Method (Middle Die)

The Balanced method represents measured, careful action where no single trait strongly applies. When you choose Balanced, you're taking a moderate approach that balances different aspects of your character.

When to Use Balanced:

  • Multiple traits have mixed relevance
  • Consistency is more important than peak success
  • You want to preserve assets and relationships
  • The situation requires careful navigation
  • You need to maintain multiple options

Strategic Considerations:

  • Balanced offers the highest chance of Partial Success (44.4%)
  • Has equal chances of Success and Setback (27.8% each)
  • Creates moderate narrative impact
  • Best used when building toward larger goals where consistency matters

Example: Balanced Method in Action

Karn needs to negotiate with a merchant from a rival clan. He chooses the Balanced method because:

  • His Origin as "Exiled Hunter" doesn't directly help with negotiation
  • His Drive to "Reclaim Honor Through Proven Strength" isn't fully applicable
  • He wants to avoid risking his "Blood-Price Token" asset
  • The situation requires navigating unfamiliar social territory

Cautious Method (Lowest Die)

The Cautious method represents careful handling of unfamiliar situations or actions that go against your character's traits. When you choose Cautious, you're acknowledging the challenge but taking a measured approach to minimize risk.

When to Use Cautious:

  • Acting against trait tendencies
  • Failure would be catastrophic
  • Assets or relationships are already strained
  • You need to preserve resources
  • The situation requires subtle action

Strategic Considerations:

  • Cautious has the highest chance of Setback (55.6%)
  • But also has a significant chance of Partial Success (44.4%)
  • Creates subtle narrative impact
  • Best used when control matters more than immediate success

Chapter 5: Game Facilitator

The Art of Facilitation

In the previous chapters, you've learned the core concepts of Narrono: how to create a setting using the Five Axes framework, how to develop characters with traits and assets, how to connect them through the Relationship Web, and how the detailed mechanics work together to create meaningful narrative moments. Now it's time to explore the role that brings all these elements together: the Game Facilitator.

As a Game Facilitator (GF), you're not just a referee or storyteller—you're a collaborative partner in creating a dynamic, evolving narrative with your players. Your role is to facilitate the story that emerges from the intersection of setting elements, character choices, mechanical outcomes, and group dynamics. This chapter will show you how to create engaging scenes, manage narrative flow, and apply the mechanics effectively to create a satisfying gameplay experience for everyone at the table.

Remember that in Narrono, facilitation isn't about controlling the story—it's about creating the conditions for a compelling collaborative narrative to emerge through play.

Core GF Responsibilities

Setting as Foundation

Every element of play builds from your setting choices. As GF, you're responsible for bringing the setting to life and showing how it shapes the characters' experiences:

  • Interpret mechanics through the setting lens
  • Connect traits to setting elements
  • Ensure assets match setting constraints
  • Create appropriate complications
  • Show how the Five Axes manifest in daily life

The setting isn't just backdrop—it's an active participant in the story. When describing scenes, always reference relevant axis elements, show setting constraints, establish context, and present setting-specific details that make the world feel alive and consistent.

Example: Setting as Foundation

In a Merit-based, Advanced Tech setting with Social Conflict, the GF describes:

"The corporate lobby's security scanners hum quietly as you enter. This description incorporates Merit-based elements (advancement through skill), Advanced Tech (security scanners, encryption), and Social Conflict (professional relationships, subtle recognition), making the setting tangible and immediate.

  • Asset usage reveals character priorities
  • Relationship involvement shows character connections

When players make mechanical choices, help them see the narrative implications. When they choose Direct method for a social challenge, it's not just about using the highest die—it's about their character confidently leveraging their noble upbringing or street smarts.

Basic Resolution Example

This example shows a simple resolution using each of the three methods (Direct, Balanced, and Cautious), demonstrating how the same situation can be approached differently.

Situation

Clara Hayes attempts to gain access to city records that might contain information about unusual incidents in Ravenshire's history. The records office is supervised by a clerk who strictly follows regulations about access.

Direct Method Example

Clara's Approach: Clara decides to leverage her Press Credentials asset and her Drive to Uncover Hidden Stories, directly asserting her journalistic authority and right to access public records.

GF: "The records clerk, a thin man with wire-rimmed glasses, looks up from his ledger as you approach. 'Records access requires proper authorization,' he states flatly. How do you approach him?"

Clara: "I'll use my Press Credentials asset and my Drive to Uncover Hidden Stories. I'll confidently present my press badge and explain that I'm researching a feature article on Ravenshire's historical development for the Chronicle's upcoming special edition. I'll be direct about my authority as a journalist to access public records."

GF: "You're using your Drive directly, so this will be a Direct method roll. Your Press Credentials asset is Fresh, so you can use it for support. Roll your three dice."

Clara rolls: 2, 4, 6

GF: "Using the Direct method, you take the highest die: 6. That's a Success! The clerk examines your credentials carefully, then nods with reluctant respect. 'The Chronicle, is it? Very well. The historical development records are in Section C. Please sign the access log.' He grants you access to the records, though you notice him making a note in a separate ledger. Your direct approach has worked, but someone will know you've been researching these records."

Balanced Method Example

Clara's Approach: Clara decides to balance her journalistic approach with some personal connection, using both her Press Credentials and her Origin as a Provincial Journalist to establish rapport with the clerk.

GF: "The records clerk, a thin man with wire-rimmed glasses, looks up from his ledger as you approach. 'Records access requires proper authorization,' he states flatly. How do you approach him?"

Clara: "I'll use my Press Credentials asset but also try to connect with him personally. I'll mention that I'm relatively new to Ravenshire, having moved from a smaller town, and that I'm still learning how things work here. I'll be respectful of his procedures while gently asserting my right to access public information."

GF: "You're balancing your professional approach with personal connection, so this will be a Balanced method roll. Your Press Credentials asset is Fresh, so you can use it for support. Roll your three dice."

Clara rolls: 2, 4, 6

GF: "Using the Balanced method, you take the middle die: 4. That's a Partial Success. The clerk softens slightly at your mention of being new to the city. Cautious Method Example

  • Fellow Veterans: Battlefield memories
  • Witnesses to Secret Event: Shared knowledge

  • Competing Heirs: Disputed inheritance
  • Ideological Opponents: Philosophical difference
  • Betrayer and Betrayed: Broken trust
  • Hunter and Quarry: Pursuit turned partnership

2. Status Establishment

Determine the current state of the relationship:

Strong

  • Full trust and cooperation
  • Reliable support
  • Clear communication
  • Shared goals or values
  • Provides full mechanical benefits

Strained

  • Underlying tensions
  • Recent disagreements
  • Conflicting priorities
  • Unresolved issues
  • Limited mechanical benefits

Broken

  • Active conflict
  • Betrayal or failure
  • Fundamental disagreement
  • Requires repair
  • No mechanical benefits until restored

Transformed

  • Recently changed relationship type
  • Evolving dynamics
  • New boundaries
  • Uncertain expectations
  • Modified mechanical benefits

3. Tension Point Identification

Identify 1-3 specific tensions that add depth and potential for development:

Internal Tensions

  • Conflicting Values: Different moral codes or priorities
  • Unequal Investment: One character more committed than the other
  • Unresolved Past: Lingering issues from shared history
  • Competing Needs: Both want different things from the relationship
  • Secret Resentment: Hidden feelings of anger or jealousy

External Tensions

  • Outside Pressures: Faction or family disapproval
  • Competing Loyalties: Obligations to others that conflict
  • Resource Scarcity: Not enough to satisfy both characters
  • Status Differences: Social or power imbalances
  • Third-Party Interference: Someone actively disrupting the bond

Setting-Based Tensions

  • Axis Conflicts: Different relationships to setting axes
  • Faction Tensions: Belonging to groups with opposing interests
  • Location Separation: Geographic challenges
  • Cultural Differences: Misunderstandings due to background
  • Historical Context: Setting history affecting the relationship

4. Development Potential

Plan how the relationship might evolve through play:

Strengthening Paths

  • Shared Adversity: Facing challenges together
  • Mutual Support: Helping each other achieve goals
  • Understanding Growth: Learning to accept differences
  • Trust Building: Proving reliability in critical moments
  • Boundary Respect: Honoring each other's limits

Complication Paths

  • Betrayal Risk: Situations that test loyalty
  • Competing Priorities: Forced to choose between relationship and other values
  • External Pressure: Others trying to drive characters apart
  • Secret Revelation: Hidden information coming to light
  • Resource Conflict: Forced to compete for limited assets

Transformation Paths

  • Role Reversal: Power dynamics shifting
  • Relationship Type Change: Evolving into a different connection
  • Mutual Growth: Both characters changing together
  • Independence Development: Becoming less codependent
  • Merging Goals: Finding common purpose

Connect the relationship to character traits for deeper integration:

Origin Connections

  • Are there shared experiences or contrasting upbringings?
  • Do their origins create natural tensions or bonds?
  • How does social structure affect their connection?

Drive Interactions

  • Do their motivations complement or conflict?
  • Can one help the other achieve their drive?
  • How do their approaches to conflict differ or align?
  • What happens when drives pull in opposite directions?

Goal Alignment

  • Are their goals compatible or contradictory?
  • Can they help each other achieve their aims?
  • What happens if one succeeds while the other fails?
  • How might goals evolve through their relationship?

  • How do their different assets complement each other?
  • What tensions might arise from asset imbalances?
  • How might assets be risked to support the relationship?

1. Position Establishment

Define the character's standing within or relationship to the faction:

Internal Positions

  • Core Member: Fully integrated, high commitment
  • Rising Member: Gaining influence, proving worth
  • Established Member: Stable position, known quantity
  • Marginal Member: On the periphery, limited involvement
  • Leadership: Decision-making authority

External Positions

  • Ally: Supportive but independent
  • Contractor: Professional relationship
  • Rival: Competitive but respectful
  • Adversary: Active opposition
  • Target: Pursued by the faction

Special Positions

  • Former Member: Left voluntarily or expelled
  • Infiltrator: Secret membership or spying
  • Debtor: Owes the faction
  • Patron: Supports the faction
  • Legacy Connection: Inherited relationship

2. Influence Mapping

Chart the bidirectional influence between character and faction:

Faction's Influence on Character

  • Resources: What assets the faction provides
  • Protection: How the faction shields the character
  • Limitations: Restrictions imposed by the relationship
  • Expectations: What the faction demands
  • Identity: How the connection shapes the character

Character's Influence on Faction

  • Contribution: What the character provides
  • Connections: Other relationships the character brings
  • Reputation: How the character affects faction standing
  • Direction: Character's impact on faction goals
  • Internal Politics: Allies and rivals within the faction

Influence Strength

  • Dominant: Overwhelming control or impact
  • Strong: Significant but not total influence
  • Moderate: Notable but limited influence
  • Weak: Minimal impact
  • Potential: Unrealized influence that could grow

3. Obligation Framework

Establish the commitments and expectations that define the relationship:

Formal Obligations

  • Contracts: Written agreements
  • Oaths: Sworn commitments
  • Debts: Resources owed
  • Positions: Official roles and duties
  • Membership Requirements: Standard expectations

Informal Obligations

  • Favors: Unwritten expectations
  • Loyalty: Expected allegiance
  • Representation: Behavior reflecting on faction
  • Support: Assistance in times of need
  • Information: Intelligence sharing

Obligation Balance

  • One-sided: Character owes much more (or less)
  • Imbalanced: Significant disparity in obligations
  • Balanced: Roughly equal commitments
  • Evolving: Shifting balance over time
  • Contested: Disagreement about obligations

4. Benefit Structure

Define what each party gains from the relationship:

Character Benefits

  • Material Resources: Equipment, funds, facilities
  • Information Access: Intelligence, knowledge, secrets
  • Status: Social standing, reputation
  • Protection: Security, backing in conflicts
  • Opportunities: Access to special situations

Faction Benefits

  • Skills/Abilities: Character's unique capabilities
  • Connections: Access to character's network
  • Representation: Character as public face
  • Deniability: Character for unofficial actions
  • Perspective: Character's unique viewpoint

Benefit Dynamics

  • Mutual Growth: Both sides prosper together
  • Zero-sum: One benefits at other's expense
  • Synergistic: Benefits multiply through cooperation
  • Diminishing Returns: Decreasing value over time
  • Potential: Unrealized benefits that could develop

5. Evolution Triggers

Identify events that could change the relationship:

Strengthening Triggers

  • Successful Collaboration: Achieving goals together

  • Resource Exchange: Mutually beneficial sharing

  • Crisis Support: Standing together in difficulty

Straining Triggers

  • Failed Obligation: Not meeting expectations

Transformation Triggers

  • Major Victory/Defeat: Significant outcome changing dynamics

  • Revelation: Discovery of new information
  • External Shift: Setting changes affecting relationship

Basic Visualization

  • Use color coding for relationship types

Digital Tools

Physical Tools

  • Repositionable notes for changing elements
  • Tokens to mark tension points

2. Connection Density Guidelines

Determine the appropriate number and distribution of relationships:

Optimal Density

  • Minimum Connections: Each character should have at least 2-3 meaningful relationships
  • Maximum Connections: Avoid more than 5-7 connections per character
  • Group Size Formula: For a group of N characters, aim for N to 2N total relationships
  • Faction Connections: Each character should have 1-2 faction relationships

Connection Distribution

  • Even Distribution: Ensure all characters have similar numbers of connections
  • Varied Types: Mix relationship types (family, professional, adversarial, etc.)
  • Varied States: Include relationships in different states (strong, strained, broken)
  • Cross-Group Connections: Ensure no character is isolated from the group

Setting-Appropriate Density

  • Power Scale Impact:
    • Personal: More intimate, fewer connections
    • Global: More extensive, complex networks
  • Social Structure Impact:
    • Tribal: Dense family/clan connections
    • Fluid: More diverse, changing connections
  • Technology Level Impact:
    • Limited: Primarily local, direct connections
    • Advanced: More distant, mediated connections

Balance Considerations

  • Avoid one character becoming the "hub" of all relationships
  • Create triangular relationship structures for dynamic interactions
  • Ensure both allies and tensions exist in the network
  • Balance character-to-character and character-to-faction connections
  • Consider how relationships might evolve over time

3. Tension Distribution

Strategically place tensions throughout the Relationship Web:

Tension Types

  • Internal Character Tensions: Conflicting goals or values within a character
  • Interpersonal Tensions: Conflicts between characters
  • Character-Faction Tensions: Conflicts between characters and organizations
  • Faction-Faction Tensions: Conflicts between organizations
  • Setting Tensions: Conflicts arising from the world itself

Tension Placement

  • Distribute Across Web: Don't concentrate all tension in one area
  • Connect to Character Goals: Link tensions to what characters care about
  • Create Triangles: Three-way tensions create dynamic situations
  • Balance with Strong Bonds: Offset tensions with supportive relationships
  • Layer Tensions: Create both surface and deeper tensions

Tension Intensity

  • Low Intensity: Background tensions that color interactions
  • Medium Intensity: Active issues that require attention
  • High Intensity: Critical conflicts that demand resolution
  • Varied Intensity: Mix different levels for dynamic play
  • Escalating Potential: Tensions that could grow more serious

Tension Triggers

  • Identify Specific Events: What might activate each tension?
  • Create Warning Signs: How will players recognize rising tension?
  • Plan Consequences: What happens if tension boils over?
  • Design Resolution Paths: How might tensions be resolved?
  • Consider Chain Reactions: How might one tension affect others?

Evaluate and adjust the Relationship Web for engaging play:

Narrative Balance

  • Spotlight Distribution: Ensure all characters have meaningful relationships
  • Story Potential: Check that relationships create interesting situations
  • Agency Balance: Verify that all players can affect the web
  • Setting Integration: Confirm relationships reflect the game world
  • Development Paths: Ensure relationships can evolve meaningfully

Mechanical Balance

  • Resource Distribution: Check that relationship benefits are fairly distributed
  • Skill Complementarity: Ensure characters can support each other's weaknesses
  • Challenge Appropriateness: Verify that tensions create suitable difficulties
  • Recovery Options: Confirm that damaged relationships can be repaired
  • System Integration: Ensure relationships interact with other game mechanics

Group Dynamic Balance

  • Cooperation Potential: Verify that characters have reasons to work together
  • Conflict Potential: Ensure tensions create interesting but not destructive conflicts
  • Independence Balance: Check that characters maintain individual agency
  • Interdependence Balance: Ensure characters need each other
  • Player Comfort: Verify that relationship dynamics respect player boundaries

Assessment Questions

  1. Does each character have meaningful connections?
  2. Are there both strong bonds and interesting tensions?
  3. Do relationships create compelling story opportunities?
  4. Is mechanical benefit distributed fairly?
  5. Does the web reflect the setting appropriately?
  6. Can all relationships evolve in interesting ways?
  7. Are there sufficient connection points for new elements?
  8. Does the web create a cohesive group dynamic?

Evolution Mechanisms

What Kind of Game is This?

Narrono is a collaborative storytelling game where you and your friends build a dramatic narrative together. Unlike games where you're simply following a predetermined story or overcoming challenges set by the Game Facilitator, in Narrono:

  • You actively shape the story through your choices both before and during play
  • Your character's decisions have meaningful consequences that ripple throughout the narrative
  • The story emerges organically from the interaction of characters, setting, and situations
  • Everyone at the table contributes to creating a compelling, evolving narrative
  • The rules exist to serve the story, providing structure and dramatic tension

In Narrono, the choices you make during character creation—selecting traits, assets, and relationships—establish who your character is and how they connect to the world. Then, during play, every decision about which traits to emphasize, which assets to risk, and which relationships to leverage further shapes the unfolding story in ways that neither you nor the Game Facilitator could have predicted.

The result is a truly collaborative experience where the Game Facilitator may set the stage, but you and your fellow players determine what happens on it. Together, you'll create a unique story that couldn't exist without your specific contributions.

Core Concepts

With that understanding of what Narrono is about, here are the key mechanical concepts that support this collaborative storytelling:

  • Characters are defined by traits that connect them to the setting
  • Actions are resolved using the Pool System (3d6)
  • Assets and relationships can be used and risked for greater effect
  • Every choice creates rippling consequences throughout the story

The Pool System

Basic Resolution

  1. Roll three six-sided dice (3d6)
  2. Choose which result to use based on your method
  3. Apply any asset or relationship effects
  4. Determine outcome level

Resolution Methods

Direct Method

  • Use the highest die
  • For actions in clear alignment with a trait
  • Higher chance of success but also higher risk

Balanced Method

  • Use the middle die
  • For actions where no traits strongly apply
  • Most consistent results

Cautious Method

  • Use the lowest die
  • For actions against trait tendencies
  • Lower chance of success but reduced risk

Understanding Results

  • Success (5-6): Achieve your intention, strengthen your position
  • Partial Success (3-4): Achieve intention with a cost or complication
  • Setback (1-2): Miss your intention, face a major complication

  • Drive: What motivates you (shaped by Conflict Type)
  • Goal: What you're trying to achieve (shaped by Power Scale)

Asset Management Guide

Assets are the equipment, abilities, resources, and connections your character can use to overcome challenges. This guide explains how to effectively manage and use assets in play.

Types of Assets

Assets come in several categories, each useful in different situations:

Physical Assets

  • Equipment, tools, weapons, vehicles, clothing
  • Tangible items your character possesses
  • Examples: Lock picks, hidden camera, family grimoire, service revolver

Social Assets

  • Connections, reputation, authority, status
  • Your standing with groups or organizations
  • Examples: Noble's network, street contacts, police badge

Knowledge Assets

  • Information, skills, education, secrets
  • What your character knows or has learned
  • Examples: Investigation notebook, family history, technical expertise

Supernatural Assets

  • Magical abilities, paranormal connections, otherworldly tools
  • Powers beyond normal human capability
  • Examples: Shadow sight, elemental control, spirit communication

Asset States

Assets exist in three states that reflect their condition and availability:

Fresh

  • Providing full benefits
  • Available for immediate use
  • No complications or limitations
  • Adds one die to your pool when used

Recovery: Fresh assets remain Fresh unless risked or degraded through story events.

Degraded

  • Creating complications but still usable
  • Limited effectiveness or reliability
  • Requires extra effort or creates side effects
  • No dice bonus when used

Recovery: Degraded assets can be restored to Fresh through:

  • Appropriate narrative actions (repairs, rest, reconnection)
  • Dedicating time and resources
  • Successful resolution of related challenges

Depleted

  • Temporarily unusable
  • Broken, exhausted, or unavailable
  • Cannot be used until recovered
  • Provides no benefits

Recovery: Depleted assets require significant effort to restore:

  • Major repairs or replacement
  • Substantial time investment
  • Resolving related story complications
  • May require a specific scene dedicated to recovery

Using Assets Strategically

When to Use Fresh Assets

  • Critical actions where success is vital
  • When the extra die could make the difference
  • When the asset perfectly suits the situation
  • When you have recovery options available

When to Risk Assets

Consider risking an asset (degrading it to reroll a die) when:

  • The initial roll would result in failure
  • The asset is particularly relevant to the action
  • You have a clear path to recovery
  • The potential success outweighs the temporary loss

Asset Recovery Planning

Always have a plan for recovering your assets:

  • Identify opportunities in the story for repairs or restoration
  • Maintain relationships that can help with recovery
  • Consider which assets are most important to keep Fresh
  • Balance risk against potential reward

Asset Risk: Marcus decides to risk his Street Contacts asset, degrading it from Fresh to Strained to reroll the 2.

Reroll: Gets a 5

  • New outcome: Success

Narration: "Marcus is losing the Keeper in the crowd when he spots a former associate. 'Create a distraction at the corner,' he whispers, slipping the man some cash. The commotion stops the Keeper just long enough for Marcus to close the gap, but word will spread that he's calling in favors in this district."

Relationship Guide

Relationships are the connections between characters that provide both opportunities and complications. This guide explains how relationships work in Narrono and how to use them effectively in play.

Understanding Relationships

Each relationship has three key components:

  1. The Parties Involved: Who is connected (usually two characters, but can include groups)
  2. The Relationship Type: The nature of the connection (professional, personal, antagonistic, etc.)
  3. The Current State: How strong or strained the relationship is at present

Relationship Types

Relationships can take many forms, each with different implications:

Personal Relationships

  • Family Ties: Connections through blood or adoption
  • Friendships: Voluntary bonds based on mutual affection
  • Romantic Connections: Intimate emotional attachments
  • Mentorships: Guidance and learning relationships

Professional Relationships

  • Authority Chains: Hierarchical connections (boss/employee, officer/subordinate)
  • Collegial Bonds: Peer relationships in work or organizations
  • Client Connections: Service provider and recipient relationships
  • Alliance Partnerships: Formal cooperation between equals

Antagonistic Relationships

  • Rivalries: Competition for the same goals or resources
  • Blackmail Bonds: Relationships based on mutual leverage
  • Vendettas: Ongoing conflicts driven by past wrongs
  • Hunter/Hunted: Pursuit relationships

Community Relationships

  • Group Memberships: Belonging to organizations or communities
  • Public Personas: Relationships with broader society
  • Factional Ties: Connections to political or social movements
  • Cultural Bonds: Shared heritage or background connections

Relationship States

Like assets, relationships exist in different states that reflect their current condition:

Strong

  • Providing full benefits and support
  • Reliable and trustworthy
  • Adds one die to your pool when leveraged
  • Creates opportunities for both parties

Example: Elena's Strong relationship with the City Council gives her reliable influence over policy decisions.

Strained

  • Limited benefits with complications
  • Requires extra effort to maintain
  • No dice bonus when used
  • May create additional challenges

Example: Marcus's Strained blackmail bond with Elena means both must tread carefully, with trust broken down and subtle threats exchanged.

Broken

  • Temporarily unusable
  • Active hostility or complete disconnection
  • Provides no benefits and may create obstacles
  • Requires significant effort to repair

Example: If Clara's relationship with her captain becomes Broken, she might face disciplinary action and lose access to police resources.

Transformed

  • Changed into a different type of relationship
  • Fundamentally altered in nature
  • May be stronger or weaker than before
  • Creates new narrative possibilities

Example: A professional mentorship might transform into a friendship, or a rivalry might transform into a reluctant alliance.

Using Relationships in Play

Leveraging Strong Relationships

When a Strong relationship is relevant to your action:

Quick Resolution Guide

This guide provides a step-by-step process for resolving actions in Narrono. Use it as a quick reference during play.

Basic Resolution Process

  1. Describe your intention

    • What are you trying to accomplish?
    • What approach are you taking?
    • What is at stake?

  2. Consider your traits

    • Which trait is most relevant to this action?
    • Is your action aligned with or against your trait tendencies?
    • How does the setting influence this action?

  3. Choose your resolution method

    • Direct Method: Use highest die when acting in alignment with a trait
    • Balanced Method: Use middle die when no traits strongly apply
    • Cautious Method: Use lowest die when acting against trait tendencies

  4. Consider assets and relationships

    • Do you have a Fresh asset that could help?
    • Is there a Strong relationship you could leverage?
    • Are you willing to risk degrading an asset or straining a relationship?

  5. Roll your dice

    • Roll three six-sided dice (3d6)
    • Apply your chosen method to select which die to use
    • Apply any modifiers from assets or relationships

  6. Determine the outcome

    • Success (5-6): You achieve your intention and strengthen your position
    • Partial Success (3-4): You achieve your intention but with a cost or complication
    • Setback (1-2): You miss your intention and face a major complication

  7. Narrate the result

    • Describe how your action plays out based on the outcome
    • Include any consequences for assets or relationships
    • Consider how this affects the ongoing story

Dynamic Outcome Interpretation (For GFs)

When interpreting outcomes, remember that while dice determine the mechanical result, you shape how these outcomes manifest narratively:

For Success (5-6)

  • Honor the mechanical success
  • Introduce future complications
  • Plant seeds for future challenges
  • Show how success changes the setting

For Partial Success (3-4)

  • Grant the intention with a cost
  • Shape the nature of the cost to drive character development
  • Use costs to introduce new story elements
  • Connect costs to existing complications

For Setback (1-2)

  • Acknowledge the mechanical failure
  • Transform failure into opportunity
  • Reveal hidden information through failure
  • Create alternative paths forward

The Compensation Principle

When dice create extreme results (multiple successes or failures in a row):

  1. Acknowledge the mechanical outcome
  2. Consider the narrative momentum
  3. Introduce balancing elements
  4. Create new opportunities

This doesn't negate the dice results but ensures the story remains engaging regardless of luck.

1. Intention: "I want to access the confidential files without leaving evidence of my intrusion."

2. Trait Consideration: Marcus's "Reformed Thief" origin is directly relevant to this lock-picking task.

3. Method Choice: Since this action aligns with his origin trait, Marcus chooses the Direct Method (highest die).

4. Asset Consideration: Marcus decides to use his Lock Picks asset (Fresh) to add a die to his pool.

  • Using Direct Method, he selects the highest die: 6

6. Outcome: Success (6) - Marcus successfully picks the lock without leaving evidence.

7. Narration: "With practiced precision, Marcus manipulates the lock picks until he feels the satisfying click of the mechanism giving way. The cabinet opens smoothly, revealing the confidential files, and no one would ever know it had been tampered with."

Ravenshire: City of Shadows

Overview

Ravenshire is a city steeped in history and secrets, where ancient noble families have ruled for centuries through a combination of political influence, wealth, and secretly wielded supernatural powers. The city presents a façade of normalcy to the world, but beneath this veneer lies a complex web of shadow magic, power struggles, and hidden agendas.

Geography

Ravenshire sits at the convergence of two rivers, surrounded by mist-shrouded hills. The city is divided into several districts:

  • The Old Quarter: Ancient stone buildings where the oldest noble families reside
  • Merchant District: The bustling commercial center of the city
  • Riverside: Working-class neighborhoods along the river
  • The Heights: Newer mansions and estates for wealthy families
  • The Depths: Poor districts and slums in the city's low-lying areas
  • The University: Center of learning and unofficial neutral ground

History

Ravenshire was founded over eight centuries ago, originally as a trading post that grew into a powerful city-state. Key historical events include:

  • The Founding (823 years ago): Establishment by the first five noble families
  • The Shadow Pact (712 years ago): Secret agreement between noble families to conceal and regulate their supernatural abilities
  • The Great Fire (256 years ago): Mysterious blaze that destroyed much of the Old Quarter, rumored to be caused by a shadow magic conflict
  • The Reform Era (90-70 years ago): Period of modernization that brought industry and reduced noble power
  • The Silent War (35-28 years ago): Undeclared conflict between noble houses that was hidden from the public

Social Structure

Society in Ravenshire is hierarchical, with clear social strata:

The Noble Houses

The descendants of the founding families who secretly possess shadow abilities:

  • House Blackwood: Masters of perception and illusion
  • House Thornfield: Controllers of shadow substance
  • House Harrington: Manipulators of emotions and minds
  • House Devereux: Wielders of shadow vitality (healing/harm)
  • House Webb: Seers of past and potential futures

The Keepers

A secretive organization that maintains the balance of shadow power:

  • Monitors noble houses for violations of the Shadow Pact
  • Recruits from both noble and common bloodlines
  • Possesses its own shadow abilities focused on detection and nullification

The Commons

Regular citizens unaware of shadow abilities:

  • Government officials
  • Merchants and professionals
  • Working class
  • The poor and disenfranchised

The Aware

Non-nobles who know about shadow abilities:

  • Trusted servants of noble houses
  • Selected government officials
  • Some academic researchers
  • Rare individuals with latent shadow sensitivity

Shadow Magic

The supernatural power that defines Ravenshire's secret society:

Source

Shadow ability stems from ancient bloodlines and awakens during adolescence. Each family has a specific affinity for different aspects of shadow manipulation.

Limitations

  • Requires concentration and line of sight
  • Cannot directly cause death (though indirect death is possible)
  • Creates a visible "shadow signature" detectable by other shadow-sensitive individuals
  • Depletes the user's energy and can cause physical deterioration if overused
  • Cannot be used on consecrated ground

Detection

Shadow use leaves traces:

  • Temporary discoloration at the site of heavy use
  • Cold spots that linger for hours
  • Distortions visible to those with shadow sensitivity
  • Emotional imprints that affect sensitive individuals

Current Situation

Tensions are rising in Ravenshire:

  • House Blackwood's patriarch has died under suspicious circumstances
  • The Keepers are investigating rumors of forbidden shadow techniques
  • Public protests against noble privilege are increasing
  • A new political movement threatens to expose corruption
  • Strange shadow phenomena have been observed in the Old Quarter

This setting provides the backdrop for the characters' adventures as they navigate the complex social hierarchy, uncover ancient secrets, and confront the consequences of centuries of shadow manipulation.

Individuals who maintain the oral history of the time before the collapse and the early struggle years. Adaptation Guidance

Pacing

For a standard campaign, this framework is designed to cover approximately 9-12 sessions:

  • Act 1: 2-3 sessions
  • Act 2: 3-4 sessions
  • Act 3: 4-5 sessions

For a shorter experience, focus on one specific clan challenge and streamline the journey phases.

Focus Areas

Depending on your group's preferences, consider emphasizing:

  • Survival: More environmental challenges and resource management
  • Clan Politics: Complex networks of relationships and loyalties
  • Exploration: Discovery of old-world secrets and technologies
  • Ethics: Moral dilemmas of community vs. individual needs in harsh conditions

Integration Tips

  • Customize clans to reflect the backgrounds and interests of the characters
  • Develop NPCs with strong clan identities and clear survival priorities
  • Create environmental challenges that showcase both the dangers and beauty of the reclaimed frontier
  • Use damaged old-world artifacts as both tools and symbols of the past
  • Emphasize how small actions can have major consequences in the fragile frontier ecosystem
